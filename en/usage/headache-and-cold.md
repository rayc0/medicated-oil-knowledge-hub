# Medicated Oils for Headache, Nasal Congestion, and the Common Cold: What the Evidence Actually Shows

> **Summary** — Topical medicated balms and oils containing menthol, camphor, and eucalyptus oil (1,8-cineole) reliably produce a *subjective* sensation of clearer breathing and calmer sleep during an upper-respiratory infection — but the best-controlled studies show little to no change in *objective* nasal airflow. The mechanism is trigeminal cold-receptor (TRPM8) activation, not decongestion. For tension headache, a topical 10% ethanolic peppermint oil solution has randomized-trial evidence comparable to paracetamol. None of these formulas should be applied to infants under 2, placed near the nostrils of small children, or used on broken skin.

## 1. Why people reach for a medicated oil when they have a cold or headache

Across East and Southeast Asia, the first response to a blocked nose, a pounding temple, or the early chills of a cold is often a small glass bottle: Tiger Balm, Wong To Yick Wood Lock, Po Sum On, White Flower, Axe Brand, Kwan Loong, Ricqlès/Double Prince peppermint water, or a generic "菜油 / 风油精." In North America and Europe, the analogous product is Vicks VapoRub.

These formulas look different on the label, but the functionally active ingredients are a small, repeating set:

| Ingredient | Typical % in a cold/headache-oriented balm | What it does on skin/mucosa |
|---|---|---|
| **Menthol** | 5 – 16 % | Activates TRPM8 cold receptors on trigeminal nerve endings → cooling sensation, subjective "airflow" feeling |
| **Camphor** | 4 – 11 % | Activates TRPA1/TRPV3, mild local anesthetic, characteristic aromatic odor |
| **Eucalyptus oil (1,8-cineole)** | 1 – 10 % | Aromatic cooling; 1,8-cineole has studied anti-inflammatory and mucolytic effects |
| **Methyl salicylate (wintergreen)** | 10 – 30 % *(only in muscle-pain-oriented balms)* | NSAID-like; not primarily used for cold/headache |
| **Peppermint oil** | 1 – 10 % *(headache balms, Ricqlès-type waters)* | Menthol-dominant essential oil; ethanolic 10 % solution has migraine/tension-headache trial data |

Understanding which of these is doing what — and what the clinical evidence actually supports — is the difference between using a medicated oil correctly and using it as a placebo that might also poison a toddler.

## 2. The key mechanism: TRPM8, not a real decongestant

The single most misunderstood fact about menthol-camphor-eucalyptus balms is this: **they do not shrink the nasal turbinates and do not measurably improve airflow through the nose**.

What they do is activate the transient receptor potential melastatin 8 (TRPM8) ion channel — the same receptor that senses environmental cold — on trigeminal nerve endings in the nasal mucosa. The brain interprets cool-feeling nasal mucosa as "air is flowing freely," even when nothing about the airway has changed [1][2][3].

Ron Eccles, a British rhinologist, documented this in a series of papers through the 1980s–2000s: inhaled menthol produces a powerful subjective sensation of clearer breathing while objective rhinomanometry shows unchanged or even slightly increased nasal resistance [2]. The 2011 Rhinology paper by Keh et al. confirmed TRPM8 immunoreactivity is densely present in human nasal mucosal nerve fibers, giving this mechanism a direct anatomical basis [3].

**Practical implication**: if someone has truly obstructive nasal congestion (for example, from turbinate swelling due to a severe URI), a menthol/camphor balm will make them *feel* better but will not actually improve the mechanics of breathing. For mechanical relief, an oral or topical α-adrenergic decongestant is the pharmacologically active choice — and the 2016 Cochrane review by Deckx et al. explicitly excluded menthol from its decongestant meta-analysis because it is not one [4].

That is not a reason to dismiss these balms. **Feeling** you can breathe and **sleeping** through the night are real clinical outcomes, especially in children, and there is a reasonable randomized trial to support them.

## 3. The Paul 2010 VapoRub trial — the best pediatric evidence we have

The most-cited piece of real evidence behind vapor rub for colds is the 2010 *Pediatrics* paper by Ian M. Paul and colleagues at Penn State, titled "Vapor Rub, Petrolatum, and No Treatment for Children With Nocturnal Cough and Cold Symptoms" [5].

**Design**: 138 children aged 2 – 11 with upper-respiratory infection symptoms were randomized to one of three arms for a single night's treatment applied at bedtime:
- **VapoRub** (camphor 4.8%, menthol 2.6%, eucalyptus oil 1.2%)
- **Petrolatum** (inactive control, same vehicle)
- **No treatment**

**Primary outcome**: parent-rated severity of nocturnal cough, congestion, and sleep difficulty.

**Result**: VapoRub was statistically superior to both petrolatum (p < 0.05 on most symptom scores) and no treatment, with the largest effect on parent-perceived child sleep quality (p = 0.006) and parent sleep quality (p = 0.008) [5].

**Limitations** (which the paper and subsequent commentary openly acknowledge):
- Unblinded — parents universally recognized the VapoRub smell.
- Single-night study — no information on whether the effect persists.
- Funded by Procter & Gamble, maker of Vicks VapoRub.
- Outcomes were parent-reported, not objective measurements.
- Children under 2 were *excluded* — deliberately, because of the well-documented risk of camphor toxicity and the FDA warning against use below that age [6][7].

A 2025 narrative review in PeerJ ("There's the Rub") walked through this and other VapoRub data and concluded that the evidence supports modest, subjective symptom relief in children ≥ 2 years — and flagged a long list of case reports of chemical pneumonitis, burns, and seizures when the product was misused (applied to the face, under the nostrils, on broken skin, or heated) [6].

**Bottom line**: in a cooperative child aged 2 or older with a cold, rubbing a small amount of a menthol–camphor–eucalyptus balm on the chest and back at bedtime has at least one decent randomized trial supporting it. That is more than can be said for most folk remedies.

## 4. What the FDA actually approves these oils for

In the United States, topical medicated oils and rubs fall under two different Over-the-Counter (OTC) monographs:

1. **21 CFR Part 341 — Cough, Cold, Allergy, Bronchodilator, and Antiasthmatic Drug Products** [7]. This monograph governs *topical antitussives for chest-rub use*. Camphor (4.7 – 5.3 %), menthol (2.6 – 2.8 %), and eucalyptus oil-based combinations are permitted as topical antitussives in ointment form. They are indicated specifically for the "temporary relief of cough due to minor throat and bronchial irritation associated with a cold," applied to the chest and throat.

2. **21 CFR Part 348 / OTC000033 — External Analgesic Drug Products** [8]. This is the monograph for muscle/joint pain rubs containing menthol, camphor, and methyl salicylate. These products are indicated for minor aches and pains — not for colds.

Two important things follow from this:

- **The FDA has *never* classified camphor, menthol, or eucalyptus oil as a true topical nasal decongestant.** It has explicitly found the evidence insufficient to put them in Category I for that use [7]. The permitted use is as a *topical antitussive* (cough suppressant by sensory effect), which is legally and pharmacologically a different claim.
- **Inhaled/steam use is regulated separately**, and the FDA has warned repeatedly that adding camphor-containing products to hot water for steam inhalation is associated with serious burn injuries in children, independent of the camphor chemistry itself.

If a product label says "clears nasal congestion" in the US market, that is a marketing claim, not an FDA-approved indication.

## 5. Headache: the peppermint-oil story

Head and temple application of peppermint-based oils (including Double Prince / Ricqlès "Alcool de Menthe," White Flower Embrocation, and Tiger Balm White) for tension-type headache is one of the most popular uses of medicated oils in Chinese households — and here the evidence is actually stronger than most physicians realize.

The landmark trial is Göbel et al., published originally in *Der Nervenarzt* in 1996 and summarized in English in subsequent reviews [9]. A 10 % ethanolic solution of peppermint oil applied to the forehead and temples in tension-type headache produced a significant reduction in pain intensity at 15 and 30 minutes, with an effect size statistically comparable to 1 g of oral paracetamol. The mechanism is again TRPM8 activation — counter-irritant cooling that modulates trigeminal pain transmission.

Subsequent work has extended this, with a 2019 systematic review finding peppermint-oil topical application to be one of the few non-pharmacological interventions with replicated randomized evidence in tension-type headache [9].

**Practical implementation** based on the original protocol:
- A light application (not a saturating dose) across the forehead and temples.
- Keep well away from the eyes — peppermint oil on the cornea is intensely painful.
- Repeat application after 15 minutes if needed.
- For migraine (as opposed to tension-type headache), evidence is weaker but the mechanism is the same; many migraine patients report subjective benefit.

Note: this evidence is specifically for a ~10 % peppermint-oil ethanol solution. Concentrated essential oils applied undiluted, or high-methyl-salicylate rubs like the muscle-pain variants of Tiger Balm, have different risk profiles and no comparable headache trial data.

## 6. How to actually use a medicated oil for cold symptoms — a step-by-step protocol

The following distills the protocol used in the Paul 2010 trial and the FDA monograph labeling language, plus the modern narrative-review recommendations [5][6][7]:

1. **Confirm the child is at least 2 years old.** Under 2 is a hard contraindication — see §7.
2. **Apply to the chest, upper back, and (optionally) the throat.** A thin layer — roughly the size of a ten-cent coin, enough to leave a glossy film. More is not better; it is a counter-irritant.
3. **Do not apply inside the nostrils, under the nose, or on the upper lip of a small child.** Both camphor and menthol are neurotoxic on mucous membranes and menthol close to the nostrils of infants has been associated with reflex apnea [6].
4. **Do not apply to broken or irritated skin, eczema, or sunburn.** The intact stratum corneum is what prevents systemic absorption of camphor to toxic levels.
5. **Do not heat and do not add to hot water for steam inhalation.** This is the single commonest source of serious burn injuries [6].
6. **Apply 15 – 30 minutes before sleep.** The Paul trial protocol used bedtime application; the subjective sensation and sleep-quality effects are maximal in that window.
7. **Wash hands after application** so the oil does not end up in the eyes.
8. **Use once per night, not repeatedly.** There is no evidence that more-frequent application helps; there is clear evidence that systemic camphor exposure scales with total dose.

For *adult* use on the chest for cough/congestion, the same rules apply, minus the under-2 concern. Adults can also use a peppermint-oil ethanolic solution for tension headache per §5.

## 7. Safety — mandatory warnings

⚠️ **The following contraindications are absolute and apply to every camphor/menthol/methyl salicylate/eucalyptus-containing medicated oil on the market.**

### 7.1 Infants and children under 2 years

Do **not** apply *any* camphor- or menthol-containing medicated oil to a child under 2. Specifically:
- **Camphor** is a GABA-A negative modulator and well-documented cause of seizures at oral doses as low as ~30 mg/kg and at topical doses when applied to broken skin or mucosa in infants. The US has banned camphor-containing preparations above 11 % since 1983, and the FDA explicitly warns against use in children under 2 [7].
- **Menthol** near the nostrils of infants has been associated with reflex apnea and bradycardia (the Javorka/Plavka observations and subsequent case reports).
- **Methyl salicylate** is the single most toxic ingredient in the class — fatal ingestions have occurred in children from as little as one teaspoon of concentrated oil of wintergreen. See our dedicated article "Methyl Salicylate Safety" for detail.

### 7.2 Pregnancy

Topical camphor and menthol in normal balm concentrations are considered low-risk in pregnancy because dermal absorption from intact skin is minimal. However:
- Avoid high-concentration camphor products and avoid large-surface application.
- Avoid peppermint oil on the abdomen in the first trimester as a precaution (no confirmed teratogenicity, but a theoretical smooth-muscle effect).
- Methyl salicylate: avoid in the third trimester on any substantial skin area — systemic salicylate carries a theoretical risk of premature ductus arteriosus closure.

See our dedicated article "Medicated Oils in Pregnancy" for trimester-by-trimester guidance.

### 7.3 G6PD deficiency (favism)

Individuals with glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency should avoid menthol-containing products, particularly in neonates where dermal menthol exposure has been associated with hemolytic crisis and neonatal hyperbilirubinemia. Camphor is also traditionally listed as a G6PD trigger in Chinese-community clinical guidance, though the mechanistic evidence is weaker than for menthol. See our dedicated article "G6PD Deficiency and Medicated Oils."

### 7.4 Broken skin, eczema, burns

Do not apply medicated oils to any broken skin surface. The stratum corneum is the rate-limiting barrier preventing toxic systemic absorption of camphor and methyl salicylate.

### 7.5 Eyes and mucous membranes

Keep all medicated oils away from eyes, mouth, nostrils, and genitals. A peppermint-oil splash to the cornea is intensely painful and produces reflex lacrimation for 20 – 40 minutes.

## 8. What medicated oils cannot do

For honesty and to keep us on the side of evidence rather than folklore, here is what topical menthol/camphor/eucalyptus products have **no convincing clinical evidence** to do:

- **Shorten a cold.** No RCT evidence.
- **Kill viruses.** In-vitro antiviral data on 1,8-cineole exist but are not clinically translated.
- **Open the Eustachian tube / treat middle-ear infection.** No evidence; ear applications are not recommended.
- **Break a fever.** Fever reduction is not a property of these balms.
- **Replace an α-adrenergic decongestant** when true mechanical congestion is the problem.
- **Treat bacterial sinusitis.** Sinusitis is diagnosed and treated separately.
- **Cure migraine.** Peppermint oil has tension-headache trial data; migraine evidence is weaker.

If cold symptoms last more than 10 days, worsen after day 5, include a high fever, or involve difficulty breathing, see a doctor. A medicated oil is a comfort measure, not a medication for serious infection.

## 9. FAQ

**Q: Can I put Tiger Balm under my nose to clear my nose?**
No. Under-the-nose application puts camphor and menthol directly on the moist upper-lip skin and within inhalation range of the nostrils, which is the zone FDA-associated with the highest adverse-event reports in children. Apply to the chest.

**Q: Is the "菜油" / "风油精" my grandmother uses the same thing?**
The basic pharmacology (menthol + camphor + sometimes methyl salicylate + eucalyptus) is similar. Concentrations vary widely and some traditional products contain significant methyl salicylate — check the label before using on children or in pregnancy.

**Q: Can I put a medicated oil in hot water and breathe the steam?**
No. This is associated with significant burn injuries and is specifically warned against [6][7]. For steam inhalation during a cold, plain hot water or saline is safer.

**Q: What about the "balms for babies" sold at pharmacies?**
Products marketed for infants typically substitute eucalyptus oil at low concentrations and remove camphor and menthol. Even so, we recommend avoiding any medicated preparation below 2 years and consulting a pediatrician.

**Q: Does putting VapoRub on the feet work?**
There is no randomized trial evidence for the "feet-and-socks" folk protocol. The Paul 2010 trial used chest and back application [5]. Do what is proven.
