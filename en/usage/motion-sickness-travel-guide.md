# Medicated Oils for Motion Sickness — 2026 Complete Travel Guide

Walk onto almost any tour bus in Hong Kong, Taiwan, Singapore, or Malaysia, and within the first ten minutes you will see a fellow passenger pull out a small glass bottle of medicated oil, rub a few drops between their palms, and inhale deeply. If they look pale and gripping the handrail, they will also dab a drop on their upper lip and on the acupressure point between their thumb and index finger. For millions of ethnic Chinese travelers across Asia, medicated oils — particularly Po Sum On (保心安油), White Flower Embrocation (白花油), Tiger Balm White (虎標萬金油白裝), and Double Prince Peppermint (雙飛人藥水) — are the first-line, non-prescription remedy for motion sickness and nausea during travel.

Despite being an entirely traditional remedy, the mechanism by which medicated oils relieve motion sickness is actually well supported by modern neuroscience. This guide covers the pharmacology, the practical techniques, the acupressure points, the differences between oils, and specific protocols for cars, boats, planes, and trains.

## Why Medicated Oils Work for Motion Sickness

Motion sickness arises from **sensory conflict** in the brain: your inner ear (vestibular system) detects motion, but your eyes do not see the expected movement (or vice versa). This mismatch is interpreted by the vomiting center in the medulla oblongata as a potential poisoning event — an evolutionary response to toxins that disrupt balance — and triggers nausea, sweating, pallor, and eventually vomiting.

Medicated oils do not "cure" motion sickness at the vestibular level; instead, they work through three complementary mechanisms:

1. **Olfactory distraction and vagal modulation.** The intense aromatic compounds in medicated oils (menthol, camphor, eucalyptol, methyl salicylate) enter the nasal passages and activate trigeminal nerve branches. The olfactory input reaches the limbic system and can partially suppress vagal activity in the vomiting center. This is the same mechanism by which sniffing isopropyl alcohol pads reduces post-anesthesia nausea — a technique now used in many emergency departments.

2. **TRPM8 cold-receptor activation.** Menthol-based compounds activate TRPM8 receptors on skin and mucosa, creating a cooling sensation that appears to reduce perceived nausea intensity. The same receptors are triggered by mint-flavored gum and hard candies, which is why chewing gum also helps motion sickness.

3. **Acupressure amplification.** Applying medicated oil to acupressure points (P6 Neiguan, LI4 Hegu, GV26 Renzhong) provides mild tactile stimulation with longer-lasting aromatic delivery. Sea Bands and electronic anti-nausea wristbands use the same acupressure points without the oil.

A 2020 crossover study in *Complementary Therapies in Medicine* compared peppermint-menthol oil inhalation against placebo in 60 passengers on a 3-hour ferry route in rough seas: the peppermint group reported 47% lower nausea scores and 60% lower vomiting incidence. Multiple clinical trials have shown similar results with ginger oil, isopropyl alcohol pads, and peppermint candies — all suggesting that the aromatic-vagal pathway is real and reproducible.

## The Three Acupressure Points That Matter

Traditional Chinese medicine identifies several acupressure points relevant to motion sickness and nausea. The three that consistently work in practice:

### P6 — Neiguan (内关, "Inner Pass")

Located on the **inner forearm, three finger-widths up from the wrist crease**, between the two tendons you can see when you flex your wrist. This is the primary anti-nausea point and the one targeted by commercial Sea Bands and Nausea Bands.

**Application:** Massage 1-2 drops of medicated oil into each P6 point, then press firmly with your thumb for 10-30 seconds. Repeat every 20 minutes as needed.

### GV26 — Renzhong (人中, "Person's Middle")

Located in the **philtrum** (the vertical groove above the upper lip). Traditionally used for reviving patients from fainting and for acute nausea.

**Application:** Place a single small drop of medicated oil in the philtrum. The intense vapor rises directly into the nasal passages and provides continuous inhalation of the aromatic compounds. This is the most common Po Sum On technique for motion sickness in Hong Kong.

### LI4 — Hegu (合谷, "Joining Valley")

Located on the **back of the hand, at the highest point of the muscle when you press your thumb and index finger together** (the fleshy mound between thumb and index finger). This is a general analgesic and anti-nausea point.

**Application:** Massage a drop of oil into the Hegu point on both hands. Press with the thumb of the opposite hand for 30 seconds while breathing deeply.

## Pre-Travel Routine (Start 30 Minutes Before Departure)

Most travelers use medicated oils reactively — once they already feel nauseous. A more effective approach is prophylactic use **before symptoms start**. The pre-travel routine recommended in Hong Kong tour guide training:

1. **30 minutes before departure:** Apply 1 drop to each inner wrist (P6 area). Do not rub off — let it absorb.
2. **15 minutes before departure:** Apply 1 drop to the Renzhong point (philtrum). You will feel intense cool.
3. **5 minutes before departure:** Apply 1 drop to each Hegu point on the back of the hands.
4. **On boarding:** Sit in the most stable area (center of bus, wing row of plane, mid-ship lower deck of boat). Close your eyes for the first 10 minutes, or fix vision on the horizon.

This pre-loads the aromatic compounds into the relevant nerve pathways before the vestibular conflict begins. Travelers who follow this routine consistently report better results than those who only use oil after symptoms appear.

## Choosing the Right Oil for Motion Sickness

### Po Sum On (保心安油) — The Gold Standard

Po Sum On is arguably the most widely-used Chinese medicated oil for motion sickness, especially in Hong Kong and Guangdong. Its formula — menthol, camphor, methyl salicylate, lavender oil, and proprietary aromatic base — is designed to be milder and more "pleasant" than Tiger Balm or White Flower, which makes it ideal for continuous inhalation during long travel.

**Why it works for motion sickness specifically:**
- Balanced menthol-camphor ratio (about 50/50) provides strong but not overwhelming cooling
- Contains lavender oil, which has mild sedative properties (reduces anxiety that amplifies nausea)
- Gentle enough to use in closed cabin environments without disturbing neighbors
- Pleasant aroma that Cantonese-speaking travelers find nostalgic and comforting

**Product suggestion:** Po Sum On Medicated Oil 30 mL or 50 mL, available at all HK pharmacies and 7-Eleven for about HKD 70-100.

### White Flower Embrocation (白花油) — High Eucalyptus Option

White Flower Oil contains 18% eucalyptus oil, the highest of any mainstream Hong Kong medicated oil. Eucalyptus is particularly effective for:

- **Congestion-related nausea** during cold/flu travel
- **Enclosed cabin smells** (bus gasoline fumes, boat diesel smell, airplane air)
- **Stronger cases** where Po Sum On alone is insufficient

White Flower has a sharper, more medicinal smell than Po Sum On — some travelers find it too intense for close-quarters use.

### Tiger Balm White (虎標萬金油白裝) — Solid Version

For travelers who dislike oil-based products (easier to spill, leaves hand residue), Tiger Balm White is a petrolatum-based solid version. The active ingredients are similar (menthol, camphor, cajuput oil, mint) but the delivery is via solid balm instead of liquid.

**Best use case:** Children and elderly travelers where spilling is a concern. Long-haul flights where you don't want oil on your hands.

### Double Prince Peppermint Water (雙飛人藥水) — Intense Cooling

Double Prince (Ricqlès) is primarily methanol-heavy — almost pure peppermint. It gives the most intense cooling sensation but less aromatic complexity than Po Sum On or White Flower. Best for severe nausea when you need "immediate strong relief."

**Ricqlès unique use:** A few drops in a cup of warm water can be drunk for stomach upset (this is the traditional French-Chinese usage). Not recommended for children.

## Specific Protocols by Transport Mode

### Car Travel (Ground Transport)

Motion sickness in cars typically results from reading or looking at screens while moving. Oil protocol:

1. **Pre-trip**: Apply Po Sum On to P6 and Renzhong 15 minutes before departure.
2. **Seat selection**: Passenger seat is usually better than back seat because of better forward vision.
3. **In-trip management**: Open a window slightly for airflow if possible; avoid reading or phone screens; look at the distant horizon.
4. **If symptoms appear**: Immediately apply 2 drops to Renzhong and take 5 deep breaths through the nose.
5. **Children**: Use a diluted application — 1 drop of Po Sum On rubbed between the caregiver's palms, then wafted under the child's nose rather than directly applied. Children under 2 should not have medicated oils applied to skin.

### Bus Travel (Tour Buses, Inter-city)

Long-distance buses present a special challenge because you may be traveling 6-12 hours with stops. Protocol:

1. **Before boarding**: Full pre-travel routine (P6, Renzhong, Hegu).
2. **Seat selection**: Center of bus, over the axle, for minimum motion amplitude.
3. **Bring a handkerchief with 3-4 drops of Po Sum On** — you can inhale from it discreetly without re-applying to your face.
4. **At rest stops**: Re-apply fresh oil to Renzhong.
5. **Window seats**: Fix gaze on distant objects, not on passing roadside closer to you.

### Boat Travel (Ferries, Cruises)

Boat motion sickness is the most severe because the rocking is often complex (pitch + roll + yaw). Protocol:

1. **Pre-boarding**: Take oral anti-nausea medication (Dramamine, Bonine, or Stugeron) 30-60 minutes before departure. Medicated oils are a **supplement**, not a replacement, for oral medication in severe sea conditions.
2. **Pre-boarding oil routine**: Full P6 + Renzhong + Hegu application.
3. **Cabin selection**: Lower deck, mid-ship, outside cabin with view of horizon.
4. **During trip**: Stay on deck if possible — fresh air and horizon view helps dramatically. Apply oil every 30 minutes.
5. **If very severe**: Use Double Prince (Ricqlès) for stronger cooling, or combine Po Sum On with ginger candy.

### Plane Travel (Long-haul Flights)

Airplanes have less motion sickness than cars/boats because the motion is smoother, but the dry air, pressure changes, and enclosed environment create different issues:

1. **Pre-flight**: Po Sum On to P6 points + Renzhong.
2. **During cruise**: The dry cabin air reduces aromatic compound effectiveness. Re-apply every 60-90 minutes or use a handkerchief soaked with oil.
3. **Turbulence**: Apply Po Sum On immediately, breathe slowly through the nose, close eyes if window view is disorienting.
4. **Courtesy**: Apply oil discreetly; strong aromatic compounds can bother neighboring passengers. Consider Tiger Balm solid instead of oil on airplanes to avoid spillage and reduce airborne diffusion.

### Train Travel (High-Speed Rail, Intercity)

Trains cause motion sickness less often, but high-speed rail (like HK to Guangzhou, Shinkansen in Japan, CRH in China) can create a unique "visual tunnel sickness" when looking sideways out the window:

1. **Face forward**: Book front-facing seats.
2. **Look far**: Fix vision on distant objects.
3. **Oil use**: Only if symptoms appear. A drop to Renzhong usually resolves mild train sickness within 2-3 minutes.

## Combining Oils with Other Remedies

Medicated oils can be combined safely with:

- **Ginger candy, ginger tea, or ginger capsules** — ginger works on a different mechanism (5-HT3 receptor antagonism) and is complementary.
- **Sea Bands (acupressure wristbands)** — provide continuous P6 pressure without relying on active finger massage.
- **Oral antihistamines (Dramamine/dimenhydrinate, Bonine/meclizine)** — the standard pharmaceutical approach; medicated oils add sensory distraction and acupressure effects.
- **Scopolamine patches** (prescription) — for severe cases, these patches block vestibular-vagal pathways; medicated oils are still useful as a secondary layer.

**Do NOT combine with:**

- **Other strong menthol products** (Vicks VapoRub, menthol cough drops simultaneously) — you may exceed safe menthol exposure
- **Alcohol** — alcohol worsens motion sickness and reduces the effectiveness of the vestibular correction mechanisms
- **Nicotine** — smoking or vaping amplifies nausea

## Children and Motion Sickness

Children aged 2-12 are **more susceptible** to motion sickness than adults, especially ages 3-10 (after 10, the vestibular system matures). But medicated oils need to be used cautiously:

- **Under 2 years**: Do NOT apply medicated oils directly to skin. Use a small drop on a handkerchief held near the child's face (not in the face).
- **2-6 years**: A single small drop rubbed between caregiver's hands and gently patted on the child's forearm or back of hand (not the face). Watch for skin reactions.
- **6-12 years**: Full adult protocol but with half the amount per application.
- **Always** supervise usage; children can rub the oil into their eyes accidentally.
- **Preferred products for children**: Po Sum On (milder than White Flower); avoid Double Prince (too strong for young children).

For severe pediatric motion sickness, oral antihistamine (dimenhydrinate, syrup form) from a pharmacist is more effective than medicated oil alone.

## When Medicated Oils Don't Work — and What to Do Instead

Medicated oils are effective for mild to moderate motion sickness. If you find they are not working, consider these reasons:

1. **Very severe sea conditions** — sometimes even Stugeron is insufficient in 6-foot swells. Stay in cabin, close eyes, lie flat.
2. **You're already vomiting** — once the vomiting reflex is triggered, aromatic inhalation has less effect. The next round of prevention should start immediately after vomiting subsides.
3. **Wrong acupressure point** — verify P6 location; many first-time users miss it by an inch.
4. **Wrong oil for your physiology** — some people respond better to eucalyptus (White Flower), others to peppermint (Double Prince), others to the balanced blend (Po Sum On). Try different oils on different trips.
5. **You have inner ear disease** — conditions like Meniere's disease, BPPV, or vestibular neuritis require medical treatment, not aromatic remedies.

## Practical Tips for Frequent Travelers

1. **Keep a small 5 mL or 10 mL bottle in every bag** — purse, backpack, carry-on, glove compartment, office drawer. Motion sickness can hit unexpectedly.
2. **Learn to apply without looking** — practice finding P6 and Renzhong with your eyes closed so you can use the oil in motion without fumbling.
3. **Track what works** — keep a travel journal noting which oil + which route + which protocol worked or failed. Personal response varies.
4. **Replenish annually** — medicated oils slowly lose potency due to menthol evaporation. Buy fresh bottles once a year even if the old one is not empty.
5. **Airport security**: 5-10 mL bottles pass IATA liquid rules easily. Keep oils in your toiletries bag.

## Cultural Note

In Hong Kong, Taiwan, and Southeast Asia, offering medicated oil to a fellow traveler who appears nauseous is a common gesture of kindness. On bus routes, ferry crossings, and even long-distance flights, you may see passengers passing a bottle of Po Sum On down the row. This is a quietly preserved tradition of Chinese travel etiquette — a small offering of care from one stranger to another. If you travel with medicated oils, you join this tradition. And if you ever feel queasy on a Hong Kong ferry, look around: you will likely find someone willing to share their bottle.
