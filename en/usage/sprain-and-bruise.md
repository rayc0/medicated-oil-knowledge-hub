# Medicated Oils for Sprains and Bruises: What Works, What Doesn't, and When

> **Summary** — For acute ankle sprains, pulled hamstrings, and soft-tissue bruising, the 2019 British Journal of Sports Medicine consensus (*Dubois & Esculier*) replaced the old RICE/PRICE acronym with **PEACE & LOVE**, which explicitly advises avoiding anti-inflammatory drugs during the first days of healing because inflammation is part of tissue repair. Medicated oils — camphor, menthol, and methyl-salicylate-based topicals such as Tiger Balm, Wong To Yick Wood Lock, Po Sum On, White Flower, and Double Prince — act mostly as **counterirritants** (menthol on TRPM8, camphor on TRPV1/TRPV3) and **rubefacients** (methyl salicylate), offering symptomatic pain relief. The most recent Cochrane review (*Derry 2014, CD007403*) concluded the evidence does **not** support topical salicylate rubefacients as disease-modifying treatment — they are comfort measures, not cures. This guide explains how to use them safely alongside the modern PEACE & LOVE protocol, with mandatory contraindications for children under 2, pregnancy, G6PD deficiency, and broken skin.

## 1. Sprain, strain, or bruise — what actually happens under the skin

Before reaching for any bottle, it matters what the injury actually is, because medicated oils are appropriate for some of these and not others.

| Injury | What's torn | Typical signs |
|---|---|---|
| **Sprain** | Ligament (bone-to-bone) | Rolled ankle, swollen joint, sometimes a "pop" |
| **Strain** | Muscle or tendon (muscle-to-bone) | Pulled hamstring, calf, or back muscle |
| **Contusion / bruise** | Capillaries and soft tissue under intact skin | Discoloration, tenderness, no joint instability |

All three are **closed soft-tissue injuries**. Medicated oils can be used on all three provided the skin is intact. They are **not** appropriate for open wounds, suspected fractures, joint dislocations, or injuries with severe deformity, inability to bear weight, or rapidly worsening swelling — those need medical evaluation, not a bottle of oil.

## 2. The modern clinical framework: PEACE & LOVE (not RICE anymore)

For decades the standard advice was RICE — Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation. In 2019 Dubois and Esculier published an editorial in the *British Journal of Sports Medicine* replacing it with two acronyms that cover the full recovery continuum: **PEACE** for the immediate days and **LOVE** for the following weeks.

### PEACE — the first few days

- **P**rotect — unload and restrict movement for 1–3 days; pain should guide you.
- **E**levate — raise the injured limb higher than the heart when possible.
- **A**void anti-inflammatories — including oral NSAIDs and ice, because inflammation helps tissue repair. This is the most-quoted change from the old RICE advice.
- **C**ompress — elastic bandage or taping to limit swelling.
- **E**ducate — set expectations; active recovery beats passive treatment.

### LOVE — from a few days onward

- **L**oad — let pain guide graduated return to movement.
- **O**ptimism — brain expectations measurably influence outcomes.
- **V**ascularisation — gentle cardio to improve blood flow to the injured area.
- **E**xercise — restore mobility, strength, and proprioception to prevent re-injury.

**Where do medicated oils fit?** They are neither protection nor load — they are a symptomatic layer *on top of* the framework. A thin application to reduce the sensation of pain can make it easier to tolerate compression, elevation, and the graduated return to load. They are not a substitute for any step of PEACE & LOVE.

## 3. What the ingredients actually do

Every mainstream Chinese or Southeast-Asian medicated oil used for sprains and bruises is a mixture of **three families** of active compounds. Understanding which family does what lets you choose rationally.

### Counterirritants (menthol, camphor)

Menthol binds the **TRPM8** cold-sensing ion channel in skin nerve endings, producing a cooling sensation and a measurable reduction in perceived pain. Camphor acts on **TRPV1** and **TRPV3** channels, producing warmth at low doses and mild local anaesthesia. Neither compound reduces tissue inflammation in any disease-modifying sense — they mask pain signals by overwhelming the same sensory pathways. This is the *counterirritant* mechanism recognised by the US FDA Tentative Final Monograph for external analgesics (21 CFR Part 348), which lists menthol (1.25–16%) and camphor (3–11%) as Category I (safe and effective) ingredients.

### Rubefacients (methyl salicylate)

Methyl salicylate, the active of wintergreen oil, causes local vasodilation — the "rub face" skin reddening — and delivers a small amount of salicylate transdermally. The 2014 Cochrane review by Derry et al. (*CD007403.pub3*) synthesised six placebo-controlled trials in acute pain (560 participants) and seven in chronic pain (489 participants) and concluded that the evidence **does not support** topical salicylate rubefacients as effective for acute or chronic musculoskeletal pain, though they are relatively well tolerated short-term. In plain English: they feel like they're doing something, but rigorous trials do not show a reliable benefit over placebo for healing. Use them for comfort, not for cure.

### Essential oils and carriers (peppermint, eucalyptus, clove, dragon-blood resin, etc.)

These contribute aroma, additional TRPM8/TRPV1 activity, and traditional indications. They are generally well tolerated on intact skin but account for most allergic contact dermatitis cases, so patch-testing on a small area first is sensible.

## 5. A realistic day-by-day usage protocol

This is a composite example of how medicated oils slot into PEACE & LOVE for a typical Grade I–II ankle sprain or calf strain on an adult with intact skin and no contraindications.

### Day 0 — the injury happens

- Sit down, remove shoe, elevate the limb.
- **Apply compression** (elastic bandage) before applying any oil — you want the bandage snug against skin, not slipping on an oily layer.
- If pain is severe, a thin, pea-sized amount of a **cool-dominant** oil (Po Sum On, White Flower, or Wood Lock) lightly massaged around — **not directly on top of** — the most swollen point can help tolerate the compression.
- **Do not apply heat packs, hot towels, or strongly warming oils** in the first 24–48 hours. Per PEACE, the goal is to not amplify bleeding into the tissue.

### Days 1–3 — the PEACE window

- Continue protection, elevation, compression.
- 2–3 times a day, re-apply a thin layer of medicated oil for symptomatic relief. Wash hands immediately afterward. Do not bandage the oiled area with plastic wrap — occlusion dramatically increases salicylate absorption and has caused fatal toxicity in reported cases (see [methyl salicylate safety](../ingredients/methyl-salicylate-safety.md)).
- Pain that is improving day-over-day is expected. Pain that is worsening, new numbness, a foot that turns dusky blue, or inability to bear any weight → seek medical review. Oil cannot fix these.

### Days 4–10 — the LOVE window begins

- Now the warming oils (Tiger Balm Red, Wong To Yick, Po Sum On) come into their own: they help with the stiffness and residual ache during graduated loading.
- Apply **before** gentle mobility work, not after — the point is to tolerate the movement, which is what actually drives healing.
- Bruise discoloration (red → purple → green → yellow) is hemoglobin breakdown and is normal. The oil does not accelerate it; gentle movement and blood flow do.

### Week 2 onward

- Oils become optional. If a specific movement still hurts after two weeks, that is a signal to see a physiotherapist, not to apply more oil.

## 6. When medicated oils are NOT appropriate

| Red flag | Why |
|---|---|
| **Open wound or broken skin** | Direct salicylate/menthol absorption can reach toxic levels; active ingredients sting and delay healing |
| **Suspected fracture** (deformity, inability to bear weight, "pop" then instability) | Needs imaging, not topical |
| **Rapidly expanding swelling or bruising beyond the injury site** | Possible vascular injury or coagulopathy — seek care |
| **Joint dislocation or visible deformity** | Emergency reduction, not topical |
| **Numbness, cold limb, or colour change distal to the injury** | Possible nerve or circulation compromise |
| **Bite wound, puncture wound, or laceration** | Infection risk; oils do not disinfect |

## 7. Mandatory safety warnings

⚠️ These restrictions are non-negotiable and apply to **every** medicated oil discussed in this article.

- **Infants and children under 2 years**: Do NOT apply camphor, menthol, methyl salicylate, or eucalyptus-based products to infants. The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP 2009; Love et al., *J Emerg Med* 2004) documents seizures, respiratory depression, and deaths from camphor exposure at doses readily reached by "just a dab." The FDA (21 CFR 310.545) has banned camphor >11% for this reason. For children 2–6, use only child-labelled formulations, never on the face, never on broken skin, and never under occlusion. Full details: [Can Babies Use Medicated Oils?](../safety/infants-and-children.md).
- **Pregnancy**: Avoid high-salicylate rubefacients (Wong To Yick, White Flower) particularly in the third trimester because of theoretical risk of premature ductus arteriosus closure and bleeding. Peppermint/menthol topicals in small amounts on intact skin are generally considered acceptable; see [Medicated Oils in Pregnancy](../safety/pregnancy-guide.md).
- **G6PD deficiency (favism)**: Menthol, camphor, and methyl salicylate are all listed on the WHO sensitising-agents list for G6PD. Haemolytic crises from topical application have been reported. Complete contraindication list: [G6PD Deficiency and Medicated Oils](../safety/g6pd-deficiency.md).
- **Broken skin, mucous membranes, eyes**: Never apply to open wounds, abrasions, burns, eyes, or inside the nose. Methyl salicylate absorption through compromised skin has caused fatal salicylate toxicity in documented cases.
- **Drug interactions**: People taking oral aspirin, warfarin, or other anticoagulants must be cautious with methyl salicylate products — systemic absorption is real and can add to bleeding risk.
- **Pets**: Cats are extremely sensitive to methyl salicylate and essential oils; do not allow cats to lick treated skin.
- **Occlusion**: Never wrap a freshly oiled area in plastic wrap or apply under a heating pad — case reports describe fatal salicylate poisoning from exactly this combination.

## 8. FAQ

**Is one brand "best" for sprains?**
No. The five mainstream brands have overlapping but different actives; the best one is whichever the user tolerates on their skin, from a verified source, with an ingredient profile that matches the injury phase. This article deliberately does not rank brands because the Cochrane evidence does not support ranking.

**Should I apply oil before or after ice?**
Per the 2019 BJSM PEACE guidance, ice is no longer a default step — inflammation is part of repair. If you do use brief cryotherapy for pain control, apply oil *after* the skin has re-warmed to avoid thermal damage.

**How many times a day can I apply it?**
FDA-labelled external analgesics permit adults to apply up to 3–4 times a day. More than that does not increase benefit and does increase systemic absorption of salicylate, which is the dose-limiting concern.

**Does the warming sensation mean it's "working"?**
It means TRPV1/TRPV3 channels and cutaneous vasodilation are being stimulated. It is not a measure of healing. Healing is driven by protected loading and blood flow, not by how hot the skin feels.

**Can I use these on a fresh bruise without a sprain?**
Yes, on intact skin. Bruises benefit most from gentle movement and time; medicated oils are symptomatic.

**Are "all-natural" herbal oils safer?**
Essential-oil-only products (no methyl salicylate) avoid the salicylate drug-interaction problem but still carry infant camphor/menthol risks and allergic contact dermatitis risk. "Natural" is not a synonym for "safe."
