## The Qingcao Herbal Tradition: Taiwan's Living Pharmacopoeia

Taiwan's approach to topical herbal medicine is inseparable from its 青草藥 (qīngcǎo yào) tradition — a centuries-old practice of using fresh or sun-dried medicinal plants for both internal and external use. This tradition was carried to Taiwan by Minnan (Hokkien) and Hakka settlers from Fujian and Guangdong provinces beginning in the 17th century under Qing dynasty expansion, and it absorbed indigenous Taiwanese plant knowledge in the centuries that followed.

The most concentrated surviving expression of this tradition is **Herb Alley (青草巷, Qingcao Xiang)**, a narrow lane running adjacent to Longshan Temple (龍山寺) in Taipei's Wanhua (萬華) district. Longshan Temple, originally built in 1738, has been a spiritual anchor for Taiwan's Hokkien community for nearly three centuries. Before Western pharmacies existed, patients would pray at the temple for healing, then step outside to consult herb vendors stationed along the temple walls — a spatial relationship between faith and medicine that persists structurally to this day.

Herb Alley reached its current density in the post-World War II decades as dispersed herb hawkers consolidated along Xichang Street Lane 224. Some shops operating today trace lineages of more than a century. The range of materials on offer is extraordinary: piles of 魚腥草 (yúxīngcǎo, *Houttuynia cordata*), fresh and dried 薄荷 (bòhé, peppermint), 艾草 (àicǎo, mugwort), 左手香 (*Plectranthus amboinicus*), and dozens of other plants that can be purchased by weight for decoctions, applied as fresh-pounded poultices, or infused in carrier oils for topical use.

Beyond Wanhua, smaller qingcao stalls and qingcao tea (青草茶) shops are scattered throughout night markets across Taiwan — from Tainan's Chikan Tower neighbourhood to the night markets of Taichung and Kaohsiung. These vendors serve a dual function: commercial herb sales and informal community health consultation, with proprietors routinely advising customers on which herbs to crush and apply topically for swelling, which to steep in sesame oil for joint ache, and which to avoid during pregnancy or infancy.

## Core TCM Concepts Behind Taiwan's Topical Practice

The therapeutic logic underpinning Taiwan's use of medicated oils centres on three classical TCM concepts that will be familiar to readers from the broader Chinese diaspora but carry distinct local flavour in Taiwan:

**祛風 (Qū Fēng — Dispelling Wind Pathogens)**
"Wind" (風邪) in TCM parlance refers to a pathogenic factor believed to invade the body via the skin and meridians, triggering headaches, stiff neck, sudden joint pain, and the early-stage shivers of a cold. Taiwanese folk practice prescribes rubbing medicated oil vigorously into the temples, neck sides, or affected joints to generate local heat, "dispersing" the wind pathogen outward through the skin. This maps mechanistically onto the TRPM8-mediated counterirritant action of menthol and camphor (McKemy 2002; Peier 2002): warming friction followed by the oil's evaporative cooling effect produces a somatosensory counterstimulus that modulates pain perception at the level of dorsal root ganglia C-fibres and Aδ-fibres.

**活血 (Huó Xuè — Activating Blood Circulation)**
"Blood stasis" (血瘀) is held to underlie bruising, post-injury swelling, and chronic dull aches. Topical oils applied with massage are believed to restore circulation to the affected area — a concept that partially aligns with the rubefacient (local vasodilatory) effects of methyl salicylate and camphor documented in the FDA external analgesic OTC monograph (21 CFR Part 348).

**通絡 (Tōng Luò — Unblocking Meridians)**
Used for chronic conditions — persistent stiffness, numbness in hands and feet, longstanding joint discomfort — this concept describes freeing blockages in the meridian (經絡) channels. In practical terms, Taiwanese users apply oils with structured massage along meridian pathways, particularly the Gallbladder (膽經) channel along the outer leg, the Governor Vessel (督脈) along the spine, and the Large Intestine (大腸經) along the arms.

## Local Brands and the Taiwanese Medicated Oil Market

### 如意油 (Rúyì Yóu / Yu Yee Oil) — The Quintessential Taiwanese Household Oil

如意油, whose name translates as "as-one-wishes oil" — invoking the auspicious *rúyì* scepter symbol of fulfilment and good fortune — is the medicated oil product most closely associated with the Taiwanese household. Its formulation typically includes peppermint oil, menthol crystals, camphor, clove oil (eugenol-rich), borneol (冰片), and in some variants nutmeg oil and cinnamon bark oil. This multi-herb combination makes it versatile: the cooling menthol/camphor layer for headaches and dizziness; the eugenol-borneol layer for light musculoskeletal aches; the peppermint for nasal clearance in motion sickness.

The brand's compact bottle format — often 15–22 ml — is designed for pocket or handbag carry, making it the preferred companion for Taiwan's extensive railway and MRT commuting culture, inter-city coach travel, and the physical demands of night-market tourism. Among Taiwan's older generation, carrying 如意油 is as habitual as carrying a phone.

**Important note on the international Yu Yee Oil brand:** The name 如意油 / Yu Yee Oil is shared by multiple manufacturers across Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Southeast Asia, producing formulations with overlapping but not identical ingredient profiles. Taiwanese domestic 如意油 products are registered under the TFDA's Chinese medicine database. When travelling internationally, consumers should verify the specific product registration in the country of purchase.

### Tiger Balm (虎標萬金油) in Taiwan

Tiger Balm's history in Taiwan mirrors its trajectory across the Chinese diaspora. Originally formulated in Rangoon by Aw Chu Kin in the late 19th century and commercialised by his sons Aw Boon Haw and Aw Boon Par from Singapore's Neil Road in the 1920s, Tiger Balm entered Taiwan during the Japanese colonial period (1895–1945) and consolidated its presence post-WWII. The red balm tin — with its iconic leaping tiger logo — became a fixture in Taiwanese household medicine cabinets during the economic austerity of the 1950s–1970s, when access to Western pharmaceuticals was limited and Tiger Balm offered broad-spectrum relief at minimal cost.

Today, Tiger Balm remains a high-visibility brand in Taiwan, particularly in airport duty-free shops and tourist-oriented souvenir markets, where its cultural symbolism as a pan-Chinese diaspora heritage product makes it popular as a gift item. Taiwanese consumers of the older generation retain strong emotional associations — Tiger Balm as a tangible link to grandparents' medicine traditions — while younger consumers tend to approach it more pragmatically as a travel convenience purchase.

### White Flower Oil (白花油 / Pak Fah Yeow) in Taiwan

White Flower Oil occupies a distinct niche in Taiwan's market: its balanced triple-counterirritant formulation (menthol 15% / methyl salicylate 15% / camphor 15%) plus eucalyptus oil (6%) and lavender (6%) positions it as a multipurpose household remedy. Older Taiwanese consumers frequently describe White Flower as the "万用药油" (all-purpose oil) and carry it as their single medicated oil solution for everything from headaches to insect bites to motion sickness. The Penang origin story — founded in 1927 by Hokkien entrepreneur Gan Geok Eng (颜玉莹) — resonates with Taiwan's own Hokkien majority population, creating a subtle ethnic-affinity connection between brand and consumer.

### Emerging Taiwanese Herbal Topical Brands

In the 21st century, Taiwanese beauty and wellness brands have begun adapting classical qingcao ingredients into premium topical formulations: essential oils and massage balms featuring 台灣牛樟芝 (Antrodia cinnamomea, a prized native fungus), 金線蓮 (Anoectochilus formosanus, a Taiwanese orchid with traditional anti-inflammatory claims), and 台灣扁柹 (Taiwan hinoki cypress oil, *Chamaecyparis obtusa* var. *formosana*). These products target urban wellness consumers willing to pay a significant premium over traditional medicated oils, reframing the qingcao tradition in the language of modern aromatherapy and evidence-based natural medicine.

## Religious and Folk Dimensions: Temple Culture and Medicated Oil

Taiwan's dense temple culture intersects with medicated oil practice in ways unique in the Chinese world.

**過爐 (Guò Lú — Passing Over the Incense Burner):** At temples dedicated to 神農大帝 (Shennong, the Divine Farmer and patron deity of medicine and pharmacology) — common throughout Taiwan — worshippers bring bottles of medicated oil or herbal preparations to be passed over the incense burner during prayer ceremonies. The act is believed to infuse the medicine with the temple's protective spiritual energy before it is taken home or given as a gift to ill friends or relatives. This ritual is not merely superstition: it embeds the medicated oil within a community network of care and intention.

**大甲媽祖遶境 (Dajia Mazu Pilgrimage):** Taiwan's most famous religious event is the nine-day, approximately 340-kilometre pilgrimage of the Dajia Mazu deity statue from Dajia (台中市大甲區) to Xingang (嘉義縣新港鄉) and back, typically held in April during the third lunar month. Hundreds of thousands of participants walk alongside the sedan chair procession. Along the route, volunteer service stations staffed by local residents provide food, water, and — critically — medicated oil for participants' feet, knees, and lower backs. For Taiwanese people, the image of a volunteer kneeling to apply medicated oil to a stranger's blistered feet is an iconic expression of the phrase "藥油即關懷" (medicated oil is care).

**家傳藥油 (Jiā Chuán Yào Yóu — Family-Transmitted Herbal Oils):** In traditional households, particularly in southern Taiwan and rural communities, grandmothers and healers maintain family-specific recipes for homemade medicated oils: mugwort (艾草) infused in sesame oil for postpartum care; ginger juice blended with camphor oil for infant abdominal massage (though see the safety warning below regarding infant use); aged 藥酒 (medicinal rice wine) extracts used for elderly joint problems. These preparations sit outside formal pharmaceutical regulation and represent a living layer of oral herbal knowledge distinct from commercial branded products.

## Regulatory Framework: TFDA and the Department of Chinese Medicine and Pharmacy

Medicated oils marketed in Taiwan for therapeutic claims are subject to two regulatory tracks:

**Track 1 — TFDA Drug Registration (中藥許可證):** The Taiwan Food and Drug Administration (TFDA, 衛生福利部食品藥物管理署, fda.gov.tw), established under the Ministry of Health and Welfare (衛生福利部), administers drug registration including traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) preparations. TCM topical products require a 中藥許可證 (Chinese medicine permit) verified through TFDA's online drug search database. Consumers can verify any product's registration status at the TFDA website.

**Track 2 — Department of Chinese Medicine and Pharmacy (DCMP, 衛生福利部中醫藥司):** The DCMP within the Ministry of Health and Welfare oversees the practice of Chinese medicine in Taiwan, maintains the Chinese Pharmacopeia of Taiwan (臺灣中藥典), and sets ingredient standards for registered Chinese herbal preparations. The Chinese Pharmacopeia of Taiwan provides monographs for individual herbal materials including menthol, camphor, and borneol that align broadly with the Chinese Pharmacopoeia (中国药典) issued by the NMPA on the mainland, while incorporating Taiwan-specific cultivation data.

Foreign brands (Tiger Balm, White Flower Oil, Wong To Yick) sold in Taiwan in therapeutic form require TFDA registration. Consumers are advised to verify the 許可證字號 (permit number) on product packaging; authentic registered products display this number in the format 衛部藥輸字第XXXXXXX號 (for imported products) or 衛部藥製字第XXXXXXX號 (for domestically manufactured products).

## Modern Evolution: Evidence-Based Herbalism and the Wellness Economy

The early 21st century has brought a measurable shift in how Taiwan's consumers engage with topical herbal medicine:

**Scientific Validation Efforts:** Several Taiwanese academic institutions and Chinese medicine research centres have published studies on the pharmacological mechanisms of classical topical ingredients — exploring menthol's TRPM8 agonism (McKemy 2002), camphor's voltage-gated sodium channel modulation, borneol's role as a transdermal penetration enhancer (Wang 2013 *J Drug Target*), and eugenol's COX-2 inhibitory effects. This research provides a bridge between traditional empirical knowledge and modern regulatory science, supporting TFDA monograph development.

**The Natural Beauty Convergence:** Taiwanese cosmetic brands have imported qingcao ingredients into skincare formulations marketed for their "herbal healing" properties — creating a premium product tier that commands 5–10× the price of traditional medicated oils while occupying the same conceptual space. Products featuring Taiwan hinoki (台灣扁柹), Antrodia cinnamomea, and alpine herb extracts are sold through boutique pharmacies, 誠品書店 (Eslite Bookstore) lifestyle retail floors, and international tourism channels, positioning Taiwan's herbal tradition as a luxury wellness export.

## Safety Warnings

⚠️ **The following safety contraindications apply regardless of cultural tradition or perceived historical safety:**

**婴幼儿 / Infants under 24 months: ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION**
Camphor, menthol, methyl salicylate, and eucalyptus oil — present in virtually all medicated oils including 如意油 and Tiger Balm — are contraindicated for children under 2 years of age. The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP 1994) and Love et al. (2004, *J Emerg Med*, PMID 15219304) document camphor seizures, respiratory depression, and fatalities in toddlers from small amounts. The FDA bans OTC camphor formulations exceeding 11% concentration (21 CFR 310.545). Paul et al. (2010, *Pediatrics*) demonstrated that camphor-menthol-eucalyptus products applied under the nose of infants under 2 worsened nasal congestion rather than relieving it.

**Traditional practices including applying medicated oil to an infant's abdomen ("搽 BB 肚") do NOT override modern pediatric evidence. Consult a registered pediatrician before applying any medicated oil to a child under 2.**

**孕婦 / Pregnant Women:**
- **First trimester:** Camphor is a known emmenagogue; avoid entirely in T1.
- **Third trimester:** Methyl salicylate (present in many medicated oils, notably White Flower Oil at 15%) can inhibit prostaglandin synthesis and theoretically promote premature closure of the ductus arteriosus. Bell & Duggin (2002, *MJA*) documented a neonatal salicylate toxicity case linked to topical methyl salicylate use in late pregnancy.
- Choose menthol-dominant, camphor-free, methyl-salicylate-free products in pregnancy (e.g., Po Sum On) only after consulting your obstetrician or midwife.

**G6PD 缺乏症 / G6PD Deficiency (蠶豆症):**
Menthol has been documented to trigger haemolytic episodes in G6PD-deficient neonates (Olowe & Ransome-Kuti 1980, *Lancet*). Camphor is also on classical G6PD trigger lists. G6PD deficiency affects approximately 2–5% of the Taiwanese male population (Taiwan Ministry of Health and Welfare neonatal screening data). All newborns in Taiwan are screened under the national neonatal screening programme (全民健康保險新生兒篩檢). If your child is known G6PD-positive, avoid all camphor- and menthol-containing topicals.

**皮膚破損 / Broken Skin:**
Do not apply to broken, abraded, burned, or inflamed skin. Morra et al. (1996, *J Rheumatol*) demonstrated 3–5× transdermal absorption of methyl salicylate under occlusive conditions; broken skin similarly amplifies absorption to potentially toxic levels.

**抗凝血藥物 / Warfarin and Anticoagulants:**
Methyl salicylate applied topically potentiates the anticoagulant effect of warfarin, as documented by Joss & LeBlond (2000, *Ann Pharmacother*, PMID 10860133, INR 12.2 case report) and Chow et al. (1989). If you or a family member takes warfarin, acenocoumarol, or a DOAC, consult your physician before using White Flower Oil, Wong To Yick, or any high-methyl-salicylate product.

**貓咪 / Cats:**
Cats lack the hepatic UGT1A6 enzyme required to metabolise phenolic compounds including menthol, camphor, and methyl salicylate (Court & Greenblatt 1997, *Drug Metab Dispos*). Even small topical exposures — from grooming a person who has applied medicated oil — can cause hepatotoxicity in cats. Keep all medicated oils stored away from cats and avoid applying them while a cat is nearby.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I buy medicated oils at Taiwan's night markets, and are they safe?**
Night market vendors and qingcao stalls sell a range of preparations with variable documentation. For therapeutic use, look for a TFDA 許可證字號 on the product label. Traditional herb preparations sold for fresh-crush external application by street vendors are generally not TFDA-registered but remain part of the qingcao tradition; use cautiously, especially on children, pregnant women, and G6PD-positive individuals.

**Q: Is 如意油 (Yu Yee Oil) safe for babies?**
No. All known 如意油 formulations contain camphor and/or menthol, both contraindicated for children under 2 years old regardless of traditional usage. The historical practice of using 如意油 for infant colic or abdominal massage reflects an era before modern toxicological evidence. Current pediatric guidance categorically prohibits camphor- and menthol-containing preparations in infants under 2.

**Q: Where can I verify whether a medicated oil product is legally registered in Taiwan?**
Visit the TFDA online drug query database at fda.gov.tw and search by product name or permit number (許可證字號). The permit number is printed on genuine registered products.

**Q: Are there Taiwan-specific medicated oil products I should look for when visiting?**
Local 如意油 (Rúyì Yóu) products from registered Taiwanese manufacturers are widely available in pharmacies (藥局) and convenience stores. Taiwan hinoki essential oil (台灣扁柹精油) and Antrodia cinnamomea (牛樟芝) formulations are available through specialty wellness shops and some airport retail outlets — these tend to be premium cosmetic rather than therapeutic products and are not subject to TFDA drug registration.

**Q: What is the difference between 如意油 and 風油精 (Fengyou Jing)?**
Both are menthol-menthol-camphor-based multipurpose medicated oils, but they have distinct origins and market positioning. 風油精 is a mainland Chinese patent medicine with a defined formulation (menthol ~32%, methyl salicylate ~26%, camphor ~3%, eucalyptus oil ~3%, chlorophyll). 如意油 is a Taiwanese product category with multiple manufacturers, each with slightly different formulations, tending toward higher clove/borneol content and a more complex herbal profile. 如意油 is regulated by TFDA in Taiwan; 風油精 is regulated by China's NMPA for mainland versions.
