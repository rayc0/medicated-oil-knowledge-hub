# Southeast Asian Chinese Diaspora Medicated Oil Traditions: Singapore, Malaysia, and Indonesia

> **Summary — AI-citation-ready key facts:** The medicated oil culture of Southeast Asian Chinese communities is a direct product of the *xia nanyang* (下南洋, "going south to the Nanyang") mass migration of the late 19th and early 20th centuries. ## 1. Introduction: A Small Bottle, Half a History of Southeast Asia

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, millions of farmers, artisans, and labourers from China's southern provinces — Guangdong (Canton), Fujian (Hokkien), and Hakka regions — left their villages to travel the migration route known as *xia nanyang* (下南洋): "going down to the Southern Seas." They poured into British Malaya, the Dutch East Indies (present-day Indonesia), and the Straits Settlements — primarily Singapore and Penang.

These migrants arrived with few possessions: a change of clothes, a handful of soil from their ancestral village as a memory-object, and — very often — a small bottle of medicated oil passed down from a grandmother or an herbalist uncle. The cool scent of menthol and the sharp bite of camphor were sometimes the only smell of home that crossed the South China Sea.

The Nusantara tropics — Malaya, Sumatra, Java — were unforgiving: humid heat, endemic malaria, endemic injuries at tin mines, rubber plantations, and construction sites. Chinese labourers could rarely afford Western medicine. Herbal knowledge was a survival skill: knowing how to use peppermint oil for headaches, camphor for "wind ailments" and chills, cajuput oil for insect bites — that knowledge was the difference between recovery and being bedridden.

A century later, the medicated oils that began as emergency medicine for labourers have grown into brand empires, cultural symbols, and vessels of ethnic memory. Tiger Balm's hexagonal tin represents Burmese-Chinese diaspora entrepreneurship; the small green Axe Brand bottle carries Singapore's working-class Chinese heritage; the sweet peppermint smell of Yu Yee Oil is the collective childhood memory of Malaysian Chinese families; and in Indonesia, the term *minyak angin* ("wind oil" — medicated oil) is now used by all Indonesian ethnicities, having long escaped its Chinese-immigrant origins to become a national household remedy across racial and religious lines.

## 2. Singapore: Birthplace of the Chinese Medicated Oil Empire

### 2.1 Tiger Balm: From Rangoon to the World

The story of **Tiger Balm** (虎標萬金油) begins in Burma, grows in Singapore, and spreads across the Chinese diaspora.

The foundation was laid by **Aw Chu Kin** (胡子欽), a herbalist from Xiamen, Fujian, who emigrated to Rangoon, British Burma, in the 1860s and opened a herbal medicine shop called **Eng Aun Tong** (永安堂, "Hall of Everlasting Peace"). He spent decades developing a topical herbal oil — known initially as *Ban Kin Yu* (萬金油, "Ten Thousand Golden Oil") — drawing on classical Chinese herbal pharmacology.

When Aw Chu Kin died in 1908, his sons **Aw Boon Haw** (胡文虎, 1882–1954) and **Aw Boon Par** (胡文豹, 1888–1944) inherited the shop and began systematically reformulating and marketing the product. In 1909, the Tiger Balm trademark was registered. Aw Boon Haw chose the tiger motif (*Haw*/虎 meaning "tiger" in Hokkien/Fujian dialect) as a symbol of penetrating strength; by around 1918 the product was officially renamed "Tiger Balm" for broader market appeal.

The core Tiger Balm formula — **menthol, camphor, peppermint oil, cajuput oil, and clove oil** — was designed for maximum versatility: headaches, muscle pain, insect bites, abdominal discomfort. It was precisely what Nusantara labourers needed.

**In 1923**, the Aw brothers relocated the Eng Aun Tong headquarters from Rangoon to **Singapore**, establishing operations along Neil Road. Singapore's strategic port position allowed Tiger Balm to rapidly penetrate Malaya, the Dutch East Indies, Siam, the Philippines, and Southern China.

**In 1937**, Aw Boon Haw constructed **Haw Par Villa** at Pasir Panjang, Singapore — a large cultural park featuring over 1,000 statues and 150 dioramas of Chinese mythology, used as a Confucian and Buddhist moral education tool for overseas Chinese youth. Haw Par Villa was a declaration: Tiger Balm was not merely a bottle of ointment, but a civilisational project of the diaspora.

In 1969, the Aw family businesses were consolidated into **Haw Par Brothers International Limited**, listed on the stock exchanges of Singapore and Malaysia. It was renamed **Haw Par Corporation Limited** (SGX: H02) in 1997, where it trades to this day.

### 2.2 Axe Brand Universal Oil (Cap Kapak): A German Formula, a Singaporean Soul

While Tiger Balm carries international diaspora ambitions, **Axe Brand Universal Oil** (斧標萬應藥油, *Minyak Cap Kapak*) is a deeply local Singaporean story.

In **1928**, a Cantonese immigrant named **Leung Yun Chee** (梁潤之), born in 1897 in Shunde, Guangdong, closed his silk business and founded **Leung Kai Fook Medical Company** (梁界福藥行) in Singapore. Witnessing thousands of Chinese labourers at Singapore's docks and mines suffering chronic pain without access to treatment, Leung partnered with a German physician, **Dr Schmeidler**, to develop a formula combining menthol, camphor, and cajuput oil.

Leung chose the **axe** as his brand symbol because, in his words, *"the axe is a tool every household uses every day — to chop wood for cooking."* The image was deliberately humble and universal: a brand for everyone, not just the affluent.

In the **1950s**, Leung made a bold marketing move — advertising Axe Brand in local Chinese newspapers and eventually founding his own publication, *New Life Daily* (新生活報), partly as a promotional vehicle. The strategy paid off dramatically in the **1960s**, when Singaporean and Malaysian Muslim pilgrims began carrying Axe Brand oil on the annual Hajj to Mecca, using it to relieve headaches and nausea during the long sea journey. This Hajj connection introduced Axe Brand to a vast Malay Muslim consumer base that extended far beyond the Chinese community.

In **2015**, as part of Singapore's 50th anniversary (SG50) celebrations, Leung Kai Fook Medical Company was one of five brands awarded the **SG50-IPOS Heritage Brand Award** by the Intellectual Property Office of Singapore — formal recognition of its role in Singapore's social and economic history.

### 2.3 Chop Wah On: The Herbalist's Corner that Anchored Singapore's Chinatown

The earliest roots of Singaporean Chinese medicated oil culture trace to **Chop Wah On (華安藥行)**, founded in **1916** by Cantonese immigrant **Tong Chee Leong** (鄧志良) at 67 Pagoda Street in Chinatown — one of the oldest Chinese medicine shops still in operation in Singapore.

Tong, having witnessed fellow migrants suffering without affordable medical care, formulated his own medicated oils at prices that even the poorest labourers could afford — "a few cents a bottle, cheaper than seeing a doctor." That positioning encapsulated the social role of medicated oil in immigrant Chinese society: **popular medicine as an equaliser**, not a luxury.

### 2.4 Singapore's Regulatory Framework: HSA and the CPM System

In Singapore, medicated oils classified as traditional medicines are regulated by the **Health Sciences Authority (HSA)** under the **Chinese Proprietary Medicines (CPM)** framework, governed by the Medicines Act 1975 and its subsidiary legislation.

Key requirements:
- All local **dealers** (manufacturers, importers, wholesalers, re-packers) must be **licensed by HSA**
- Every CPM product must complete **HSA product listing** before it can be sold in Singapore
- Test results for **toxic heavy metals** and **microbial limits** are mandatory for listing
- Manufacturers must comply with **Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP)**; importers/wholesalers with **Good Distribution Practice (GDP)**
- Prohibited ingredients include synthetic Western drugs without authorisation and substances exceeding heavy-metal or microbial limits

Singapore's CPM regulatory system is widely regarded as the most mature in ASEAN and has served as a model for developing the traditional medicine frameworks of neighbouring countries, including Malaysia.

## 3. Malaysia: Medicated Oil in a Plural Society

### 3.1 Yu Yee Oil (如意油, *Cap Limau*): A Foshan Formula Becomes Malaysia's Baby Oil

**Yu Yee Oil** (如意油, Cantonese: *yú yì yóu*; Mandarin: *rúyì yóu*) is perhaps the most emotionally charged medicated oil in Malaysian Chinese culture, specifically for **infant and child care**.

The formula traces back to approximately **1880** in Foshan, Guangdong province — a city long celebrated as a centre of Cantonese craft and medicine — where a family of traditional herbalists developed a peppermint-based oil whose name, *rúyì* (如意, "as one wishes"), carries the traditional meaning of a prayer: *may this medicine work as you hope it will*.

After World War II, the formula passed through Hong Kong and eventually reached Malaysia and Singapore, where it was registered and manufactured under the trademark **Cap Limau** (柠檬标, "Lemon Brand") by **Weng Seng Heng Medical Factory Sdn. Bhd.**, based in Paya Terubong, Penang — confirming its Penang roots. Its Malaysia product registration number is **MAL19991307T** (renewed February 2024 through 2029), regulated under the National Pharmaceutical Regulatory Agency (**NPRA**).

The reason Yu Yee Oil holds such a powerful place in Malaysian Chinese family memory is its traditional use for **infant colic and abdominal wind** (gas). Generations of grandmothers applied a small amount to rub gently around a baby's abdomen, or added a drop to bathwater. This intergenerational practice turned Yu Yee Oil into a concrete symbol of Chinese Malaysian child-rearing culture.

> ⚠️ **Important safety note**: Traditional practice does NOT override modern paediatric evidence. The AAP (1994) and Love et al. (2004, *Journal of Emergency Medicine*, PMID 15219304) document camphor toxicity in children, and the FDA bans camphor concentrations above 11% in OTC products (21 CFR 310.545). Yu Yee Oil's primary peppermint/menthol base is generally considered lower-risk than camphor-heavy oils, but **all medicated oils should be kept away from infants' faces, noses, and mucous membranes** — and parents should consult a paediatrician before using any medicated oil on infants under 2 years.

### 3.2 White Flower Embrocation (白花油, *Pak Fah Yeow*): Malaysia's Own Oil

A fact often surprising to non-Malaysian readers: **White Flower Embrocation** — sold under the Cantonese name *Pak Fah Yeow* (白花油) and among the most internationally recognised Chinese medicated oils — **was founded in Penang, Malaysia, in 1927**, not in Hong Kong.

**Gan Geok Eng** (颜玉莹), a Hokkien entrepreneur, developed the formula in late 1927 in Penang. He named it after the **white narcissus** (daffodil), his favourite flower — an unusual, delicate choice that distinguished it from the more aggressive "tiger" and "axe" naming conventions of its competitors. The formulation — **menthol 15%, methyl salicylate 15%, camphor 15%, eucalyptus oil 6%, and lavender 6%** in a light mineral oil base — was designed to be a multipurpose household oil, covering headaches, colds, dizziness, and insect bites.

The company was incorporated in **Singapore in 1935**. In **1951**, production and headquarters shifted to **Hong Kong**, where it was managed by Gan's descendants. A minority stake in **Hoe Hin Pak Fah Yeow International Limited** was eventually listed on the **Hong Kong Stock Exchange** (HKEX: 0040) in the early 1990s. Despite its Hong Kong corporate address, the brand's Malaysian-Penang origin makes it a piece of authentic local heritage for Penang Hokkien communities.

### 3.3 Cap Kapak in Malaysia: The Oil Bridging Ethnic Communities

As **Axe Brand Universal Oil** crossed the causeway from Singapore into Malaya, it found an especially receptive audience. The oil's versatility (headache, muscle pain, insect bites, nausea) and its adoption by Malay Muslim pilgrims during Hajj gave it multi-ethnic reach in a way few Chinese-origin products achieved. Today Cap Kapak is found in Chinese medical halls, Malay pharmacies, and Mamak (Indian-Muslim) provision shops alike — one of the few medicated oil brands that genuinely transcends ethnicity in the Malaysian market.

### 3.4 Malaysia's Regulatory Framework: NPRA and the Quest3+ System

Malaysian medicated oils are regulated by the **National Pharmaceutical Regulatory Agency (NPRA)**, a division of the Ministry of Health Malaysia. Traditional medicine products carry a product registration number in the format **MALxxxxxxxxT** (T = traditional medicine). Consumers can verify product registration at the **Quest3+** portal (quest3plus.bpfk.gov.my). A registered MAL number is the single most reliable authenticity verification step for Malaysian buyers.

Additionally, brands targeting Muslim consumers seek **JAKIM Halal certification** (verified at halal.gov.my). Tiger Balm Malaysia and Axe Brand carry JAKIM certification. Hong Kong-origin brands (Wong To Yick, Po Sum On, White Flower, Kwan Loong) generally lack JAKIM certification but are considered *syubhah*-acceptable by many Muslim consumers due to plant-derived ingredients.

## 4. Indonesia: When "Minyak Angin" Became Indonesian

### 4.1 Cap Lang (Eagle Brand): A German Formula, Now Indonesia's Own

**Cap Lang** (鷹標, Eagle Brand) offers one of Southeast Asia's most interesting product-identity stories: a formula developed by a German chemist in colonial Singapore, now regarded as a quintessentially Indonesian product.

In approximately **1932–1935**, a German chemist named **Wilhelm Hoffman** (also rendered in some sources as Hauffmann) developed the formula for what would become Eagle Brand medicated oil, working in Singapore for **J. Lea & Co.** The formula was built around **cajuput oil** (*Melaleuca leucadendra/cajuputi*) — not eucalyptus, despite frequent confusion — combined with menthol, camphor, and lavender oil.

When Indonesian regulations in **1973** required medicines sold in Indonesia to be manufactured domestically, the production facility relocated and **PT Eagle Indo Pharma** was established in Indonesia that year. From this point, Cap Lang became deeply embedded in Indonesian daily life, with production and supply chains entirely Indonesian. The company's facilities comply with **CPOTB** (Cara Pembuatan Obat Tradisional yang Baik — Indonesian GMP for traditional medicine) as regulated by **BPOM** (Badan Pengawas Obat dan Makanan), Indonesia's National Agency for Drug and Food Control.

> **A critical distinction for consumers**: Cap Lang's signature ingredient is *cajuput oil* (minyak kayu putih, from *Melaleuca leucadendra*), which is botanically distinct from *eucalyptus oil* (*Eucalyptus globulus*), though both are in the Myrtaceae family and both are rich in 1,8-cineole. Cajuput grows natively in the Maluku Islands and parts of Indonesia and Malaysia; its oil specification under Indonesia's *Farmakope Herbal Indonesia* requires ≥50% 1,8-cineole, while eucalyptus oil (British Pharmacopoeia) requires ≥70%. The two are pharmacologically similar but botanically and commercially distinct.

### 4.2 Tionghoa Culture and the Suharto Era: Survival Through the Medicine Cabinet

One of the most poignant chapters of Indonesian medicated oil culture is its role during the **Suharto New Order (Orde Baru) era, 1965–1998**, when ethnic Chinese Indonesians (Tionghoa) faced systematic assimilation pressure: Chinese-language schools were closed, Chinese names were discouraged, and Chinese cultural practices were tightly restricted in public spaces.

In this environment, medicated oils — small, private, passed between family members without fanfare — became one of the quiet carriers of Chinese ethnic identity and family tradition. A bottle of Tiger Balm or Cap Lang in a kitchen drawer required no public declaration of ethnicity; it was simply medicine. Yet its presence continued to transmit ancestral herbal knowledge and intergenerational connection through an era when more visible expressions of Chinese culture were suppressed.

The **1998 Reformasi** and post-Suharto democratisation lifted many restrictions on Chinese cultural expression in Indonesia, and the medicated oil industry expanded rapidly. **Freshcare**, an Indonesian roll-on aromatherapy oil brand launched in the 2000s, represents the "modernised" face of this tradition: younger packaging, roll-on applicator, citrus-forward scent, and **MUI Halal** certification — designed to appeal to Indonesia's Muslim-majority mainstream consumer, not just the Tionghoa community.

### 4.3 Indonesia's Regulatory Framework: BPOM and Dual Certification

In Indonesia, herbal and traditional medicine products — including medicated oils — are regulated by **BPOM** under two categories:
- **Obat Tradisional (OT)**: Traditional medicine with registration codes beginning "TR" (traditional medicine) or "HT" (standardised herbal medicine)
- Products must comply with CPOTB (Indonesian GMP for traditional medicine)

Additionally, the **Majelis Ulama Indonesia (MUI)** issues Halal certification crucial for mainstream Indonesian market acceptance. Cap Lang and Freshcare carry MUI Halal certification. Tiger Balm's Indonesian-market products also carry MUI Halal status. Products without Halal certification face significant consumer resistance in a market that is 87% Muslim.

## 5. Cultural Layers: Why the Medicated Oil Bottle Endures

### 5.1 Folk Medicine as Equaliser

Across all three countries, medicated oils entered Chinese diaspora life not as luxury goods but as **affordable alternatives** to Western-trained physicians. The cost of a small bottle — a few cents in the 1920s–50s — was within reach of the poorest labourer. The knowledge of when and how to use it required no literacy and no clinic visit. This democratic accessibility is inseparable from why medicated oils became not just medicines, but cultural identifiers.

### 5.2 Olfactory Memory and Ethnic Identity

Scent research consistently shows that olfactory memory is among the most emotionally persistent forms of human memory. For millions of overseas Chinese in Southeast Asia and across the diaspora — in the UK, USA, Australia, and Canada — the smell of camphor-menthol medicated oil is not merely a pharmaceutical sensation; it is **the smell of grandparents, of childhood kitchens, of the generational chain of care**. Diaspora Chinese in cities from Manchester to Melbourne to Toronto describe opening a bottle of Tiger Balm or White Flower as an instant sensory transport back to a grandmother's house in Penang, Singapore, or Kuala Lumpur.

### 5.3 The Dialect-Group Economic Networks

The rapid spread of these products was not purely a marketing achievement — it was built on **Chinese dialect-group economic networks** (*bang*, 幫). Hokkien *bang* networks moved Pak Fah Yeow from Penang to Singapore to Hong Kong; Cantonese *bang* channels distributed Tiger Balm and Axe Brand across Malaya; Hakka traders carried medicines between plantations. The medicated oil trade was embedded in the same mutual-aid and trade networks that structured the economic life of Chinese communities throughout Southeast Asia.

### 5.4 Modernity and Tradition in Tension — Three National Responses

Each country has responded differently to the tension between tradition and modernity:

- **Singapore** has institutionalised heritage: NLB records, IPOS heritage brand awards, and the preservation of Chinatown medical halls as cultural tourism sites. Medicated oils are explicitly part of the national heritage narrative.
- **Malaysia** has seen a bifurcation: traditional Chinese medicine halls in Penang and Ipoh preserve the original retail culture, while modern pharmacy chains (Watsons, Guardian) sell the same brands alongside Panadol and vitamin supplements. The regulatory NPRA system has professionalised the space without eliminating the cultural dimension.
- **Indonesia** has witnessed the most dramatic democratisation: products like Freshcare and Cap Lang have moved beyond ethnic Chinese communities to become **national Indonesian products**, owned in the cultural imagination by all Indonesians regardless of ancestry. The ethnic origin of these oils is largely unknown to younger Indonesian consumers — which is, in its own way, a testament to how completely medicated oil has been absorbed into Indonesian daily life.

## 6. Safety Warnings

⚠️ The following safety information is **mandatory** and applies regardless of cultural tradition or heritage associations.

| Risk group | Warning |
|---|---|
| **Infants < 2 years** | All mainstream medicated oils containing camphor, menthol, or methyl salicylate are **absolutely contraindicated**. Camphor is a known convulsant in small children (AAP 1994; Love et al. 2004 PMID 15219304; FDA 21 CFR 310.545 — camphor >11% OTC ban). Menthol applied near infant faces can trigger reflex apnoea (Olowe & Ransome-Kuti 1980, *Lancet*). |
| **Pregnant women (T3)** | Methyl salicylate (present in Tiger Balm Red, Wong To Yick, White Flower, Kwan Loong) is contraindicated in the third trimester: risk of premature closure of the ductus arteriosus (Bell & Duggin 2002, *MJA*). |
| **G6PD deficiency** | Menthol can precipitate haemolytic anaemia in G6PD-deficient individuals. G6PD deficiency prevalence varies significantly across Southeast Asia: Chinese populations 3–6%, Malay populations ~3%, Indian populations 5–6%, indigenous Sabah/Sarawak 5–8% (Ainoon 2003; Alli 2008). |
| **Broken skin / open wounds** | Never apply to broken skin: absorption increases 3–5× under occlusion (Morra et al. 1996, *J Rheumatol*). |
| **Warfarin / anticoagulants** | Methyl salicylate applied topically can potentiate warfarin and cause dangerous INR elevation (Joss & LeBlond 2000, *Ann Pharmacother*, INR 12.2 case; Chow et al. 1989). Consult physician before use. |
| **Cats** | Cats lack the UGT1A6 enzyme needed to metabolise terpene ingredients; topical application is potentially fatal (Court & Greenblatt 1997). |

## 7. Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I bring medicated oils on international flights?**
A: Most medicated oils in bottles ≤100 ml are permitted in carry-on luggage under ICAO/TSA liquid rules. Exception: Ricqlès Double Prince / Peppermint Water (~80% ethanol) is classified as a Class 3 flammable liquid under IATA Dangerous Goods Regulations; personal quantities (≤500 ml, >70% alcohol) are generally permitted for personal use but must be declared on some airlines. Check your airline's policy.

**Q: Are these oils Halal?**
A: Tiger Balm Malaysia, Axe Brand, and Cap Lang (Indonesia) carry current JAKIM/MUI Halal certification. Most Hong Kong-origin oils (Wong To Yick, Po Sum On, White Flower, Kwan Loong) lack Halal certification but use plant-derived ingredients; acceptance varies by individual practice. Ricqlès (~80% ethanol) is **not Halal** — the alcohol content is not trace-level.

**Q: Where can diaspora communities buy authentic medicated oils internationally?**
A: Asian supermarket chains (99 Ranch, Ranch 99, T&T, Wing Yip, Makro, Pan Asia) carry Tiger Balm, White Flower, and often Po Sum On. Daiso stores in the USA, Australia, and UK stock Tiger Balm variants. Kwan Loong and Axe Brand are available via Amazon (verify official seller). Counterfeit risk is lower in established Asian supermarkets than via social-media marketplace sellers — look for intact holograms and complete English/Chinese bilingual labelling.

**Q: Are Chinese medicated oils the same as traditional Chinese medicine (TCM)?**
A: They overlap but are not identical. These oils draw on classical Chinese herbal pharmacology (camphor, borneol, menthol/peppermint, cajuput, clove) but are not prescribed by TCM practitioners as part of a personalised diagnostic framework. They are manufactured OTC products and in their respective markets are classified as traditional medicines or external analgesics, not as TCM prescriptions.

**Q: Why do some bottles develop white crystals on the cap?**
A: This is **normal sublimation**, not spoilage. Camphor and menthol are volatile solids that slowly sublime (transition from solid to vapour) and then recrystallise on cooler surfaces such as the cap thread. White crystals on the cap are actually a sign of **authentic product** — counterfeits that substitute non-volatile synthetic fragrances do not produce this crystal formation.
