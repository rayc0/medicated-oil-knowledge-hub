## 1. Historical Roots: Kampo and External Medicine {#1-historical-roots}

Japan's tradition of topical therapeutic preparations is inseparable from **Kampo medicine** (漢方, *Kanpō*) — the Japanese adaptation of traditional Chinese medicine that arrived via the Korean Peninsula during the 5th and 6th centuries CE. In 753 CE, the Tang monk Jianzhen (鑑真) arrived in Nara carrying 36 categories of medicinal herbs, among them materials used for external poultices (*貼り薬*, *harigusuri*).

During the **Edo period (1603–1868)**, Japanese physicians systematically refined external preparations, selecting approximately 300 herbs from the Chinese pharmacopoeia and adapting them to Japan's unique climate and constitution. Hot-spring (*onsen*) culture reinforced the cultural value of applying heat and topical substances to the body for pain relief — a practice that would later shape consumer acceptance of modern medicated patches.

The **Meiji government's 1874 medical reform** — which mandated Western medicine and effectively banned Kampo from the formal healthcare system — pushed traditional external medicine out of hospitals but kept it alive in households and apothecaries (*薬局*). By the early 20th century, Japanese pharmaceutical entrepreneurs fused this grassroots tradition with industrial chemistry, producing the modern medicated plaster category.

### The Poultice Tradition

The classical Japanese poultice (*膏薬*, *kōyaku*) was made from herbs — camphor (樟脑), menthol from peppermint, and warming spices — mixed with a starch base and applied to linen or Japanese paper. This format was the direct ancestor of modern paper-backed medicated patches. The body's occlusive contact with the patch base amplifies transdermal absorption by 3–5× compared with open-air application, a pharmacokinetic principle later quantified in modern research (Morra et al. 1996).

## 2. Salonpas: From Kyushu Poultice to Global #1 Patch {#2-salonpas}

### Company History

**Hisamitsu Pharmaceutical Co., Inc.** (久光製薬) was founded in **1847** as *Komatsuya* in Tosu, Saga Prefecture, Kyushu — a region with a centuries-old tradition of herbal medicine linked to proximity to Nagasaki, Japan's sole trading port during the Edo period. The company's early products were rubbing ointments (*擦り薬*) sold to samurai households and later to the general public.

In **1903**, Hisamitsu developed *Asahi Mankinko* — a prototype therapeutic poultice in which rubbing ointment was pasted onto Japanese paper. This was the direct technological ancestor of Salonpas.

### Salonpas Launch and Global Expansion

| Milestone | Year |
|---|---|
| Komatsuya apothecary founded, Tosu, Saga, Kyushu | 1847 |
| Asahi Mankinko prototype poultice developed | 1903 |
| Salonpas commercially launched in Japan | 1934 |
| First overseas exports | 1937 |
| Taiwan subsidiary established | 1960 |
| Indonesia joint venture | 1975 |
| US FDA OTC approval (Salonpas Pain Relief Patch + Arthritis Pain) | 2008 |
| Recognised world's #1 OTC topical analgesic patch (Euromonitor) for 8 consecutive years | 2016–present |
| Present in countries/regions | 50+ |

The Salonpas Pain Relief Patch formulation contains **methyl salicylate** (methyl ester of salicylic acid, also known as wintergreen oil) as the primary active ingredient, supported by **l-menthol** and **dl-camphor** — the same trio found in many traditional Asian medicated oils. Hisamitsu's innovation was delivery mechanism: embedding these actives in a polymer matrix that adheres to skin and releases ingredients at a controlled rate over hours, rather than the rapid-then-dissipating profile of rubbed-on oils.

### Transdermal Drug Delivery Innovation

Hisamitsu's investment in **transdermal drug delivery systems (TDDS)** extended beyond OTC analgesics. The company later developed prescription hormone patches (estradiol), cardiovascular patches (nitroglycerin, tulobuterol bronchodilator), and dementia patches (rivastigmine), making it a global TDDS pharmaceutical leader with Japan as the originating market. The OTC Salonpas line is, historically, the product that funded this research platform.

## 3. Tokuhon: Japan's Other Long-Running Plaster Icon {#3-tokuhon}

**Tokuhon Corporation** (徳本) was founded in **1901** and named after **Dr. Nagata Tokuhon** — a celebrated physician of the late Muromachi to early Edo period (~500 years ago) known for his expertise in external medicine. Their flagship medicated plaster has been sold since **1933** and is present in nearly every Japanese household medicine cabinet.

### Tokuhon Formulation

Tokuhon's classic formulation contains:

| Ingredient | Role |
|---|---|
| Methyl salicylate | Primary anti-inflammatory / analgesic |
| 1-Menthol (l-menthol) | Cooling sensation, TRPM8 agonist |
| dl-Camphor | Counterirritant, mild analgesic |
| Glycyrrhetinic acid | Topical anti-inflammatory (licorice-derived) |
| Vitamin E acetate (tocopherol acetate) | Promotes local circulation |

This formulation mirrors the active-ingredient philosophy of liquid Asian medicated oils — camphor + menthol + methyl salicylate — but in a slow-release adhesive patch format. The same core actives appear in Tiger Balm Red (camphor-dominant), Wong To Yick Wood Lock Oil (methyl salicylate-dominant at ~40%), and Kwan Loong Oil (balanced menthol + methyl salicylate).

## 4. Active Ingredients in Japanese Topical Analgesics {#4-active-ingredients}

Japanese topical analgesic products — whether oil, balm, or patch — predominantly rely on five pharmacologically established active ingredients:

### Methyl Salicylate (メチルサリチル酸 / 冬青油)

- **Mechanism**: Inhibits prostaglandin synthesis (COX pathway) at the local tissue level; counterirritant stimulating cutaneous sensory fibres
- **Regulatory status**: Listed in JP18 (日本薬局方 第18改正); US FDA OTC Monograph for external analgesic (21 CFR Part 348); upper limit 10% without prescription in most Japanese formulations
- **Key toxicity note**: Methyl salicylate is a significant oral toxin — 5 ml of pure methyl salicylate (~6 g of salicylate) can be lethal in a small child. One teaspoon of wintergreen oil is equivalent to ~23 adult aspirin tablets. See Safety Warnings below.
- **Concentration in common products**: Salonpas patch ~10%, Tokuhon plaster ~10–15%, Wong To Yick Wood Lock Oil (sold at Japanese drugstores) ~40%

### l-Menthol (l-メントール)

- **Mechanism**: Selective TRPM8 (transient receptor potential melastatin 8) channel agonist — produces perceived cooling without actual temperature change (McKemy et al. 2002, *Nature* 416:52–58; Peier et al. 2002, *Cell* 108:705–715)
- **Regulatory status**: JP18 monograph; FDA OTC external analgesic 1.25–16% Category I (21 CFR Part 348)
- **Safety note**: G6PD-deficient individuals and infants under 2 years require special caution (see below)

### dl-Camphor (dl-カンフル)

- **Mechanism**: TRPV1 agonist (warming); TRPM8 agonist at low concentrations (cooling); mild local anaesthetic at nerve terminals
- **Regulatory status**: JP18; FDA OTC 3–11% Category I (21 CFR 310.545 — >11% camphor in OTC products is banned by FDA)
- **Critical safety note**: Camphor is a potential CNS convulsant in children, causing seizures at doses as low as 1 g. The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP 1994) advises against any camphor use in children.

### Borneol (ボルネオール / 冰片)

- **Mechanism**: Mild skin penetration enhancer; GABA-A modulator; weak TRPM8 agonist
- **Source**: Present in some Kampo-derived preparations and traditional imported oils
- **Note**: Natural borneol (right-handed *D*-borneol) from *Dryobalanops aromatica* is extremely rare; nearly all commercial borneol is synthetic racemic mixture

### Methyl Salicylate vs. Ketoprofen Patches

A major regulatory distinction between Japan and other countries: Japan has a large market for **topical NSAID patches** (diclofenac, ketoprofen, flurbiprofen, indomethacin) — prescription and OTC — which have higher clinical evidence levels for musculoskeletal pain than counterirritant-based OTC patches. These are separate from the traditional camphor/menthol/methyl-salicylate OTC category. The **Cochrane Review (Derry et al. 2012, CD009403)** found topical NSAIDs effective for acute musculoskeletal pain with low systemic adverse-effect risk.

### Regulatory Note for Japan

Most imported Asian medicated oils enter Japan as **general goods** or **quasi-drugs** (医薬部外品, *iyaku-buigai-hin*) rather than as classified pharmaceuticals. Japanese customs may restrict quantities. Products registered as pharmaceuticals in their origin country (HK CMRO pCm classification; SG HSA CPM) are **not automatically registered in Japan** under the Pharmaceutical and Medical Device Act.

For personal-use import (個人輸入), Japanese law generally permits import of up to the equivalent of approximately one to two months' personal supply without formal drug registration — but the importer bears responsibility for compliance. Commercial import and retail sale of unregistered foreign pharmaceuticals requires formal registration under the Yakujiho.

## 7. Regulatory Framework {#7-regulatory}

### Pharmaceutical and Medical Device Act (薬機法 *Yakujiho*)

Japan's overarching pharmaceutical law — formally the **Act on Securing Quality, Efficacy and Safety of Products Including Pharmaceuticals and Medical Devices** — governs all pharmaceuticals sold in Japan. Topical analgesics fall into three categories:

| Category | Japanese | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Prescription drug** (医療用医薬品) | 処方箋医薬品 | Requires physician prescription | Ketoprofen patch >35mg, diclofenac gel |
| **OTC drug — Class 1/2** (要指導医薬品 / 第一類/第二類) | 薬局医薬品 | Sold only at pharmacies with pharmacist supervision | Tokuhon Loxy (Class 2 ketoprofen-containing) |
| **OTC drug — Class 3** (第三類) | 第三類医薬品 | Sold at pharmacies and drugstores without mandatory consultation | Standard Salonpas, Tokuhon standard |
| **Quasi-drug** (医薬部外品) | 医薬部外品 | Cosmetic/general wellness claim, lower regulatory bar | Many cooling body sprays, muscle-conditioning products |

### Japanese Pharmacopoeia 18th Edition (JP18)

The **JP18** (日本薬局方 第18改正) came into effect on **June 7, 2021**, published by the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare (MHLW, 厚生労働省) and administered by the **PMDA** (Pharmaceuticals and Medical Devices Agency, 医薬品医療機器総合機構). JP18 includes monographs for:
- Methyl Salicylate (サリチル酸メチル)
- Menthol (メントール, l-form)
- Camphor (カンフル / 天然カンフル / dl-カンフル)
- Borneol (ボルネオール)
- Eucalyptus oil (ユーカリ油)

Products using these ingredients as actives must comply with JP18 purity and specification standards.

### Quality Verification

Consumers can verify that a Japanese OTC drug is legitimately registered by checking the **registration number** on packaging against PMDA's pharmaceutical database at [pmda.go.jp](https://www.pmda.go.jp/). Look for a 承認番号 (*shōnin bangō*, approval number) on the product carton.

## 8. 安全警示 / Safety Warnings {#8-safety-warnings}

⚠️ **These warnings apply to all topical medicated oils and patches containing camphor, menthol, methyl salicylate, or borneol.**

### Infants and Children Under 2 Years — Absolute Contraindication

- **Do not use** any camphor-containing product on children under 2 years. Camphor is rapidly absorbed through infant skin and mucous membranes and can cause **seizures, CNS depression, and respiratory arrest** even at small doses (American Academy of Pediatrics, 1994; Love et al. 2004, *J Emerg Med* PMID 15219304).
- **Menthol** on infant skin or near the face can cause apnoea and bronchospasm. The Paul et al. (2010, *Pediatrics*) VapoRub RCT found camphor/menthol/eucalyptus products **worsened** respiratory symptoms in children under 2.
- **Eucalyptus oil**: 41-case pediatric toxicity series in Australia (Tibballs 1995, *MJA*) documented serious toxicity in children after minor exposure.
- **Japanese emergency**: ☎ **119** (ambulance) · ☎ **#7119** (nurse hotline, metropolitan Tokyo) · 中毒情報センター **072-727-2499** (Osaka) / **029-852-9999** (Tsukuba) — available 24 hours.

### Pregnancy

| Trimester | Camphor | Methyl Salicylate | Menthol |
|---|---|---|---|
| First trimester (T1) | Avoid — animal teratogenicity data | Avoid — prostaglandin inhibitor, early closure risk | Low risk; precautionary avoidance preferred |
| Second trimester (T2) | Limit to small areas, brief contact | Small-area application, no occlusion | Generally considered low risk topically |
| Third trimester (T3) | Avoid near term | **Contraindicated** — risk of premature closure of ductus arteriosus (Bell & Duggin 2002, *MJA*) — equivalent to NSAID effect | Low risk topically; avoid high-concentration inhalation |

**Note for Wong To Yick users in Japan**: This product contains ~40% methyl salicylate — the highest concentration among commonly available Asian medicated oils. In the third trimester, this product must be avoided entirely. Even in earlier pregnancy, application should be confined to a small area (≤5 cm × 5 cm) with no occlusion.

### G6PD Deficiency (蚕豆症 / G6PD欠乏症)

- **Menthol** can cause haemolytic jaundice in individuals with G6PD deficiency, particularly neonates (Olowe & Ransome-Kuti 1980, *Lancet* 2:969).
- G6PD prevalence in Japan is relatively low (~0.1–0.3% estimated), but is higher in ethnic-Chinese and ethnic-Southeast-Asian communities resident in Japan.
- Parents of G6PD-positive neonates should consult a paediatrician before using any menthol-containing product.

### Warfarin and Anticoagulant Interaction

- Topical **methyl salicylate** can potentiate the anticoagulant effect of **warfarin** (Coumadin), raising INR to dangerous levels. A case report documented INR of 12.2 after extensive topical methyl salicylate application in a patient on warfarin (Joss & LeBlond 2000, *Ann Pharmacother* PMID 10860133).
- **Wong To Yick** (40% methyl salicylate) carries the highest risk; Salonpas (~10% methyl salicylate) lower risk.
- If you take warfarin, acenocoumarol, or any anticoagulant: consult your physician before using any methyl-salicylate-containing topical product.

### Broken or Damaged Skin

Do not apply any medicated oil or patch to broken, abraded, sunburned, or eczematous skin. Morra et al. (1996, *J Rheumatol*) demonstrated 3–5× increased methyl salicylate systemic absorption under occlusion — damaged skin removes the barrier further and dramatically raises absorption to potentially toxic systemic levels.

### Cats and Pets

Cats cannot metabolise camphor, menthol, methyl salicylate, or borneol effectively due to deficiency of UDP-glucuronosyltransferase (UGT) enzymes (Court & Greenblatt 1997). Do not apply these products near cats and store them out of reach of pets.

## 9. FAQ {#9-faq}

**Q: Is Salonpas the same formula worldwide?**

A: The core OTC Salonpas formula (methyl salicylate + l-menthol + dl-camphor) is broadly consistent, but active concentrations vary slightly by market to comply with local regulations. The US formulation was specifically approved by FDA OTC review in 2008. In Japan, the domestic formulation complies with JP18 and the Yakujiho.

**Q: Can I buy Japanese prescription-strength ketoprofen patches as a tourist?**

A: No. Prescription-only (第一類医薬品 requiring pharmacist dispensing with some formulations, or 処方箋医薬品) topical NSAIDs require a Japanese physician's prescription. Standard OTC Salonpas and Tokuhon are freely available without prescription.

**Q: Is it safe to take multiple packs of Salonpas home in my luggage?**

A: For personal use, this is generally permitted. There are no IATA flammability restrictions on standard Salonpas patches (unlike Ricqlès/双飛人 which is 80% ethanol and classified as a flammable liquid). However, IATA and customs rules may restrict large commercial quantities. Ricqlès-type alcohol-based medicated preparations should comply with the ≤100 ml per container hand-luggage limit.

**Q: I have a warfarin prescription. Can I use Salonpas?**

A: Caution is warranted. Even at 10% methyl salicylate, extensive application of multiple patches daily can raise systemic salicylate levels enough to affect INR. Use the minimum effective number of patches, do not occlude, and have your INR monitored more frequently when starting topical analgesic use. Wong To Yick (40% methyl salicylate) should be avoided on warfarin.

**Q: What is the difference between Tiger Balm Red and Tiger Balm White for Japanese users?**

A: Tiger Balm **Red** (camphor 25%, menthol 10%) is intended for deep muscle pain, stiff shoulders (*肩こり*), and bruises — warming in character. Tiger Balm **White** (camphor 11%, menthol 8%, peppermint oil) is lighter and cooler, suited to headache (apply to temples), cold congestion, and insect bites. Both contain camphor and must not be used on children under 2.

**Q: Are Wong To Yick or other HK medicated oils registered as drugs in Japan?**

A: Generally, no. Most Asian import medicated oils sold in Japanese Chinatown shops or online are imported as general merchandise or personal-use quantities rather than as registered Japanese pharmaceuticals. They are not regulated under the Yakujiho registration process. If you require a registered drug, choose a Japanese domestic brand with a 承認番号.
