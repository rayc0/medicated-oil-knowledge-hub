# South Korea's Medicated Oil and Topical Analgesic Tradition: From Joseon-Era Hanbang to the Modern K-Health Market

> **Summary** — South Korea's topical medicine tradition is rooted in **Hanbang** (한의학, Korean traditional medicine), a system derived from classical Chinese medicine and independently developed over 1,600 years. The country's pharmaceutical history includes the world's oldest registered pharmaceutical product, **Whal Myung Su** (활명수, 1897, Dongwha Pharm), and a contemporary consumer market defined by medicated patches (파스), mugwort-based preparations, and a growing global K-health export sector. Asian import medicated oils — Tiger Balm, White Flower, Wong To Yick — are available through Olive Young and duty-free retail, while MFDS (식품의약품안전처, Ministry of Food and Drug Safety) regulates external drugs under the Korean Pharmacopoeia 12th Edition (KP12).

## 1. Historical Roots: Hanbang and the Joseon Pharmacopoeias {#1-historical-roots}

### The Arrival of East Asian Medicine in Korea

Korean traditional medicine — known as **Hanbang** (한방, 韓方) or **Hanuihak** (한의학, 韓醫學) — descends from classical Chinese medicine (TCM) transmitted to the Korean Peninsula via the Han Chinese commanderies and, later, the Buddhist networks of the Three Kingdoms period (57 BCE–668 CE). However, what arrived in Korea was rapidly adapted to local ecology, materia medica, and constitutional theory, producing a distinctly Korean medical tradition.

The earliest systematic Korean medical text, **《향약구급방》** (*Hyangyak Gugeupbang*, "Emergency Remedies Using Local Medicines"), was compiled during the Goryeo Dynasty (c. 1236 CE) and catalogued 180 native Korean medicinal plants — including external-use preparations for bruises, burns, and joint pain. This document represented the first formal break from full dependence on Chinese-imported medicines, asserting that Korean indigenous herbs (*hyangyak*, 향약) were pharmacologically sufficient for most conditions.

### The Joseon Pharmaceutical Canon: 《동의보감》

The apex of classical Korean medical scholarship arrived in 1613 CE with **《동의보감》** (*Dongui Bogam*, "Principles and Practice of Eastern Medicine"), compiled by physician **Heo Jun** (허준, 1539–1615) under royal commission from King Seonjo. A UNESCO Memory of the World inscribed text (2009), *Dongui Bogam* devoted significant chapters to **외형편** (External Form Volume) and **잡병편** (Miscellaneous Diseases Volume), both of which include topical preparations:

- **고약 (膏藥, *goyak*)**: Oil-based medicated plasters, prepared by heating herbal materials in sesame oil until the volatile compounds were extracted, then blending with beeswax to form a solid-paste application
- **산제외용 (散劑外用)**: Powdered herb preparations moistened with vinegar, ginger juice, or rice wine for topical application to bruises and sprains
- **훈법 (薰法)**: Fumigation therapy where aromatic herbs (including camphor analogues) were burned to release vapours for inhalation and surface-level therapeutic effect

The emphasis on **temperature-activating therapeutics** — warm oils, heated plasters, moxibustion — established a cultural predisposition toward thermally-enhanced topical medicine that persists in Korean consumer preferences today.

### Japanese Colonial Period and Pharmaceutical Modernisation

During the Japanese colonial period (1910–1945), Korean traditional medicine was systematically suppressed in favour of Western biomedicine. However, as documented by historian Park Yunjae and colleagues in a 2023 PMC study ("Medicine of the Grassroots"), the *hyangyak* tradition survived in the commercial sphere: traditional herbal vendors adapted their products to new regulatory categories, often framing them as "digestive drinks" or "health tonics" rather than medicine. This survival strategy produced some of Korea's most enduring pharmaceutical brands.

## 2. Whal Myung Su: Asia's Oldest Living Pharmaceutical Brand (1897) {#2-whal-myung-su}

### Foundation and Guinness Recognition

In **1897**, court military officer **Min Byung Ho** (민병호) combined court herbal prescriptions with Western pharmacological principles to develop **활명수** (*Whal Myung Su*, 活命水 — literally "life-saving water"). He established **Dong Wha Yak Bang** (동화약방, precursor to Dongwha Pharm) the same year.

Whal Myung Su holds **four Guinness World Records**:
1. Oldest manufacturing company in the Republic of Korea (est. 25 September 1897)
2. Oldest pharmaceutical company in the Republic of Korea (est. 25 September 1897)
3. Oldest registered trademark in the Republic of Korea (registered 15 August 1910)
4. Oldest registered product in the Republic of Korea (*Whal Myung Su*, registered 16 December 1910)

### Formulation and Function

Whal Myung Su is an oral digestive medicine rather than a topical oil — it is formulated from:
- **Aquilaria agallocha** (침향, *chimhyang*) — aromatic resinous heartwood used in TCM and Hanbang for warming qi and settling the stomach
- **Cinnamomum cassia** (계피, *gyepi*) — cinnamon bark, carminative and antimicrobial
- **Syzygium aromaticum** (정향, *jeonghyang*) — dried cloves, digestive tonic
- **Catechu** (아선약) — astringent; anti-diarrhoeal
- Ethanol and sodium bicarbonate as vehicle

Its relevance to the medicated-oil tradition lies in the aromatic-terpene profile: the same families of plant compounds — cinnamon oils, clove eugenol, volatile resins — that define Whal Myung Su have always appeared in Korean topical preparations. The boundary between "internal aromatic medicine" and "external aromatic medicine" in classical Hanbang was fundamentally porous.

Today, approximately **100 million bottles** are sold annually in Korea (Dongwha Pharm annual report data), with around KRW 46 billion (≈ USD 34 million) in annual sales, representing 70% of the Korean digestive liquid market (Korea.net, 2023).

## 3. Mugwort (쑥 / Ssuk) and External Medicine {#3-mugwort}

### Artemisia princeps in Korean Culture

**쑥** (*ssuk*, *Artemisia princeps*) occupies a unique position in Korean botanical culture: it appears in the founding myth of Korea (Gojoseon, c. 2333 BCE, in which a bear consumes ssuk and garlic to become human), in spring cuisine (ssuk rice cakes, *ssuk guk* soup), and pervasively in Hanbang medicine as both an internal remedy and external therapeutic agent.

In external medicine, ssuk's primary application is **뜸 (*tteum*, moxibustion)**: the dried leaf wool (moxa, 애엽 *aeyeop*) is pressed into cones or cigar-shaped rolls and burned at acupuncture points on the skin surface. The thermal stimulus plus the volatile components of *Artemisia* — including **cineole, camphor, thujone, artemisinin, and borneol** — are thought to penetrate superficially and stimulate local circulation.

### Evidence Base for Moxibustion

The clinical evidence for moxibustion is limited and mixed:

| Indication | Evidence Level | Key Citation |
|---|---|---|
| Breech presentation correction | Moderate (Cochrane 2023) | Coyle 2023 Cochrane CD003928 |
| Knee osteoarthritis pain | Low quality (heterogeneous trials) | Lee 2010 Acupunct Med |
| Chemotherapy-induced nausea | Insufficient evidence | — |
| General analgesia | Preclinical data only | — |

The Cochrane 2023 Systematic Review on moxibustion (Coyle et al., CD003928) noted that while some evidence suggests moxibustion may reduce breech presentation, the safety profile was poorly reported, and adverse effects (burns, allergic reactions to smoke) require attention.

For topical product purposes, the most relevant mechanism is the **direct transdermal delivery of ssuk volatile compounds** when mugwort preparations are used as poultices or creams — not via burning but via occlusive skin contact. This is the basis for the growing K-beauty mugwort skincare sector (brands: COSRX, Innisfree, Dr. Jart+).

## 4. 파스 Culture: Korea's Medicated Patch Revolution {#4-pas-culture}

### From Japanese *Harigusuri* to Korean 파스

The word **파스** (*pas*, from the Japanese *pasupasusu* or "Parsons" — the brand name of an early import) has become the generic Korean term for all medicated patches and plasters. Korea inherited the medicated patch format from Japan's Meiji-era pharmaceutical industry, but subsequently built an independent domestic market that now rivals Japan's on a per-capita basis.

### Market Structure

Korean consumers spend an estimated **KRW 1.8 trillion+ annually** on external analgesic preparations (patches, ointments, gels, and oils combined), driven by:

- **Office worker culture**: Prolonged desk work, screen use, and commuting create high demand for neck-shoulder and lumbar patches
- **스포츠 파스 (sports patches)**: Youth and adult sports participation drives demand for fast-acting menthol/camphor patches
- **어르신 파스 (elderly patches)**: An ageing population with high osteoarthritis prevalence represents the largest growth segment
- **편의점 파스 (convenience store patches)**: CU, GS25, and 7-Eleven all stock 파스 alongside beverages — a uniquely Korean retail phenomenon

## 5. Active Ingredients in Korean Topical Products {#5-active-ingredients}

Korean topical medicines use the same core counterirritant and anti-inflammatory molecules found globally, regulated under **KP12** and **MFDS OTC Drug Standards** (의약품 기준 및 시험 방법):

| Ingredient | KP12 Status | Concentration Range (Typical OTC) | Primary Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| 장뇌 (Camphor, *Cinnamomum camphora*) | KP12 monograph | 3–11% topical OTC | TRPV1/TRPA1 activation; counterirritant (FDA 21 CFR 348) |
| 멘톨 (Menthol, *l*-menthol) | KP12 monograph | 1.25–16% topical OTC | TRPM8 agonist; cooling-substitution analgesia |
| 살리실산메틸 (Methyl salicylate) | KP12 monograph | 10–30% in patches/oils | COX inhibition (transdermal NSAID-like) + counterirritant |
| 케토프로펜 (Ketoprofen) | Prescription→OTC reclassified | 30mg/patch | COX-1/COX-2 non-selective NSAID |
| 디클로페낙 (Diclofenac) | Prescription | 1% gel/patch | COX-2 preferential NSAID |

The MFDS classifies topical products in three tiers:
1. **의약품 (Pharmaceutical drugs)**: Require MFDS approval; may make therapeutic claims
2. **의약외품 (Quasi-drugs)**: Lower-risk categories including some herbal preparations; require registration but lighter clinical data burden
3. **화장품 (Cosmetics)**: No therapeutic claims permitted; functional cosmetics require MFDS "기능성화장품" filing

Most Asian import medicated oils (Tiger Balm, White Flower, etc.) enter Korea as **의약외품** or as personal-import items in tourist retail, not as registered 의약품.

## 6. Asian Import Medicated Oils in Korea {#6-asian-imports}

### Tiger Balm (호랑이 밤 / 타이거밤)

Tiger Balm is the most recognisable Asian medicated oil among Korean consumers. It is sold at:
- **Olive Young** (올리브영) — Korea's leading health-and-beauty chain, ~1,300 stores nationwide
- **Lotte Mart / E-Mart** international-product aisles
- **Airport duty-free** (Incheon, Gimpo): a popular souvenir purchase for Korean travellers returning from Singapore or Hong Kong

Korean consumers primarily use Tiger Balm for headache (관자놀이/temple application) and mosquito bites — consistent with the brand's global positioning. The name is commonly romanised on Korean retail shelves as both **타이거밤** and **호랑이 밤**.

### Wong To Yick Wood Lock Oil (황도익 활락유)

Wong To Yick's 40% methyl salicylate formula is found primarily in:
- 화교 슈퍼 (Chinese-community grocery stores) in **대림동** (Daerim-dong, Yeongdeungpo, Seoul) and **인천 차이나타운** (Incheon Chinatown)
- Some Korean-Chinese herbal medicine shops (한방 약재상)

Health Canada has classified products with >30% methyl salicylate as prescription-only; Korea's MFDS has not issued an equivalent restriction as of KP12 (2022), but Wong To Yick occupies a grey area: it is not MFDS-registered and is typically sold as a personal import or through informal channels. Korean consumers, particularly those from Chinese-diaspora families, are familiar with its high-MS potency for muscle injuries.

### White Flower Embrocation (백화유 / 하얀꽃 오일)

White Flower Oil (Hoe Hin Pak Fah Yeow, HKEX: 0040), with its distinctive lavender-eucalyptus scent profile, is sold in selected Olive Young branches and at K-pop fan-market tourist shops. Its multipurpose positioning (headache, cold, insect bite, muscle) aligns well with the Korean convenience-product preference.

### Kwan Loong Oil (관롱 오일)

Primarily circulates in Korean-Chinese community networks. Less widely distributed than Tiger Balm in mainstream retail.

### Po Sum On (보심안유)

Occasionally available in specialty Korean-Chinese herbal medicine retailers. Its camphor-free, menthol-heavy formula makes it a frequent recommendation for G6PD-affected consumers who need to avoid the methyl-salicylate oils. The absence of camphor and methyl salicylate is a meaningful distinction given the MFDS OTC limits on both compounds.

## 8. Regulatory Framework: MFDS, KP12, and Hanbang Drug Classification {#8-regulatory}

### Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (MFDS / 식품의약품안전처)

The **MFDS** (website: mfds.go.kr) is Korea's equivalent of the US FDA. For topical analgesic products:

- **Pharmaceutical drugs (의약품)**: Must be registered in the MFDS NEDRUG database (nedrug.mfds.go.kr). All Korean-manufactured topical analgesics (Salonpas, Ketotop, etc.) are registered here.
- **OTC drug standards**: MFDS publishes specific concentration limits for camphor (≤11%), menthol, and methyl salicylate in OTC topical products, broadly aligned with FDA 21 CFR 310.545 and 21 CFR 348.
- **Import drugs**: Foreign medicated oils imported for direct retail sale require MFDS drug registration. Most Asian import oils sold in tourist/duty-free contexts are not MFDS-registered; personal importation of small quantities for personal use is generally tolerated.

### Korean Pharmacopoeia 12th Edition (KP12 / 대한민국약전 12개정)

**KP12** (effective 2022, published by MFDS) is the official drug standards compendium. It includes:
- Monographs for camphor (*Camphora* 장뇌), menthol (*Mentholum* 멘톨), and methyl salicylate (*Methylis Salicylas* 살리실산메틸)
- Standards for topical preparations (외용액제, 외용제)
- Purity, assay, and storage specifications harmonised with JP18 and USP-NF

The KP12 camphor monograph specifies purity ≥96% and distinguishes between natural (천연 장뇌, from *Cinnamomum camphora*) and synthetic (합성 장뇌) forms — consistent with the Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2020 dual-monograph approach.

### Korean Hanbang Drug Regulation

Hanbang medicines are regulated as a distinct category under **한약서 (Korean Herbal Pharmacopoeia)**, a companion document to KP12. External-use Hanbang preparations (고약, 외용 한약) must comply with KHP standards for quality and must not make unsubstantiated claims. The dual regulatory track (biomedicine + Hanbang) means Korean consumers encounter both KP12-compliant products and KHP-compliant products on the same pharmacy shelf.

## 9. 안전 경고 / Safety Warnings {#9-safety-warnings}

⚠️ **These warnings apply to all medicated oils and topical analgesics containing camphor, menthol, or methyl salicylate — regardless of whether the product is Korean-made or an Asian import.**

### 영아·유아 (Infants and Children Under 2 Years)

**절대 금지 (Absolute contraindication)** for any product containing camphor ≥1%, menthol, or methyl salicylate applied to children under 24 months. This includes all major import medicated oils (Tiger Balm, White Flower, Wong To Yick, Kwan Loong, Po Sum On, Whal Myung Su herbal preparations).

- Camphor is rapidly absorbed through infant skin and can cause **seizures and respiratory failure** at topical doses that would be safe for adults. Accidental ingestion of as little as 1 teaspoon of camphor oil can be fatal in a toddler (Love et al. 2004, PMID 15219304; AAP Committee on Drugs 1994).
- Menthol applied to the face or chest of infants has been associated with **apnoea and respiratory distress** (Paul et al. 2010 VapoRub RCT, *Pediatrics*). This applies to the Korean 멘톨 파스 category as well.
- Methyl salicylate is concentrated in the Wong To Yick formula (40%); accidental ingestion of a few millilitres can cause **salicylate toxicity** in children (Davis 2007, *J Emerg Med*; Bell & Duggin 2002, *MJA*).

**If a child is exposed**: Call 중독정보센터 (Poison Information Center) **☎ 1339** (24 hours) or **☎ 119** immediately. Do NOT induce vomiting.

### 임산부 (Pregnant Women)

- **Camphor**: Crosses the placental barrier; associated with neonatal toxicity in case reports. The FDA has set an OTC maximum of 11% camphor in topical preparations (21 CFR 310.545), and the Chinese Pharmacopoeia 2020 labels borneol (a camphor-related compound) as 임부 禁用 (contraindicated in pregnancy). Avoid camphor-containing oils in all trimesters.
- **Methyl salicylate**: In the third trimester, topical methyl salicylate poses a risk of **premature ductus arteriosus constriction** via prostaglandin (COX) inhibition — the same mechanism as systemic NSAIDs. This risk is highest in T3 and parallels ACOG 2015 guidance banning oral NSAIDs after 20 weeks gestation (Torloni et al. 2006, PMID 16355417; Bell & Duggin 2002).
- **Methyl salicylate (T1–T2)**: Minimise use; avoid large-area or occluded application (Morra et al. 1996 documented 3–5× absorption increase under occlusion).
- **Alternatives**: Plain heat packs (non-medicated 핫팩) and gentle massage are generally considered safer in pregnancy. Consult 마더세이프 전문상담센터 **☎ 1588-7309** (Mon–Fri, Korea's national prenatal drug consultation service).

### G6PD 결핍 (G6PD Deficiency / Favism)

G6PD deficiency affects approximately 0.2% of Korean males (lower than Southeast Asian and Chinese populations, but not negligible). Menthol has been associated with haemolytic anaemia in G6PD-deficient infants (Olowe & Ransome-Kuti 1980, *Lancet*).

- Avoid menthol-containing products in known G6PD-deficient individuals, particularly infants and young children.
- Po Sum On (보심안유, contains 15–22% menthol) should be used with caution in this population.

### 와파린 / 항응고제 복용 중 (Warfarin / Anticoagulant Users)

Wong To Yick (황도익 활락유) and other high-methyl-salicylate products can **potentiate warfarin anticoagulation**, leading to dangerously elevated INR values. A documented case reported an INR rise to 12.2 following methyl salicylate topical application in a warfarin patient (Joss & LeBlond 2000, *Ann Pharmacother*, PMID 10860133). Users of warfarin, rivaroxaban, apixaban, or other anticoagulants should consult their physician before using any product containing >10% methyl salicylate.

### 손상된 피부 (Broken or Inflamed Skin)

**Never apply** medicated oils or patches to broken skin, open wounds, sunburned skin, or skin affected by eczema or dermatitis. Absorption increases 3–5× under occlusion compared with intact, non-occluded skin (Morra et al. 1996, *J Rheumatol*). Patches applied over broken skin constitute an overdose risk.

### 반려동물 주의 (Pets — Especially Cats)

Cats lack the UGT1A6 hepatic enzyme required to metabolise camphor, menthol, and methyl salicylate. Topical exposure or licking of medicated oil from human skin can cause **acute liver failure** in cats (Court & Greenblatt 1997, *Drug Metab Dispos*). Store all medicated oils out of reach of pets.

## 10. FAQ {#10-faq}

**Q: Can I buy Korean 파스 (Ketotop, Trast) outside Korea?**
A: Yes. Ketotop (ketoprofen patch) and similar NSAID patches are popular "pharmacy souvenirs" from Korea and are widely available at Korean supermarkets in major diaspora cities and online via Korean international shipping services. Note that Ketotop is a prescription-only product in some countries (notably Canada and UK, where ketoprofen topicals require a prescription). Methyl salicylate-based patches (Salonpas, Sinsin Pas) are generally OTC-exportable.

**Q: Is Tiger Balm registered with MFDS in Korea?**
A: Tiger Balm is generally available through retail channels in Korea as an imported personal-use item and through duty-free. It is not broadly MFDS-registered as a 의약품 (pharmaceutical drug) for direct OTC retail. However, it may appear as a 의약외품 (quasi-drug) through specific distributor registrations. For verified regulatory status, search the MFDS NEDRUG database at nedrug.mfds.go.kr using the product name.

**Q: What is the Korean 중독정보센터 (Poison Information Center) number?**
A: **☎ 1339** (24 hours, operated by the Korea Institute of Drug Safety & Risk Management / 한국의약품안전관리원). This line handles ingestion and overdose queries for medicated oils, 파스, and herbal preparations. Emergency: ☎ 119.

**Q: Can Korean 뜸 (moxibustion) practitioners use commercial medicated oils as a substitute?**
A: No. 뜸 involves direct thermal stimulation of acupuncture points via burning *Artemisia* moxa — the therapeutic rationale is fundamentally different from counterirritant topical oils. A practitioner trained in Korean moxibustion may use topical oils adjunctively to prepare the skin or to follow up after needling/moxibustion, but they are not therapeutically interchangeable.

**Q: Is Whal Myung Su (활명수) safe to use as a topical preparation?**
A: Whal Myung Su is formulated as an **oral digestive liquid**, not a topical product. Do not apply it to skin. Its aromatic herbal ingredients (clove oil, cinnamon oil) could cause contact sensitisation if used topically, and the ethanol vehicle is not suitable for skin application in the concentrations present in the oral formula.

**Q: What do Korean hanbang clinics (한의원) typically use for topical external therapy?**
A: Hanbang (Korean traditional medicine) practitioners at registered 한의원 (clinics) use a combination of: (1) 고약 (*goyak*) — traditional oil-based herbal plasters; (2) 첩포 (herbal poultices, often mugwort/ginger-based); (3) 침 (acupuncture) with topical oils used to facilitate needling; (4) 뜸 (moxibustion). Commercial medicated oils like Tiger Balm may be used informally between sessions but are not part of formalised Hanbang protocol.
